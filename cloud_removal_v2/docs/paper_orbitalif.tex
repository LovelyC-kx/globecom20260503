\begin{abstract}
Satellite networks can acquire massive space information with advanced
remote sensing technologies, providing critical support for real-time
applications such as natural disaster monitoring. Emerging Space
Computing Power Networks provide a new architecture for coordinating
onboard computing resources, enabling satellites to perform intelligent
in-orbit processing collaboratively. Nevertheless, optical cloud
removal is a high-fidelity dense restoration task, and existing
artificial-neural-network-based models usually incur high computation
and energy costs, making them difficult to sustain under the limited
power supply and thermal conditions of satellite platforms.
To tackle this issue, we propose \OrbitALIF{}, which exploits the
sparse event-driven computation of spiking neural networks to reduce
inference energy, while enhancing pixel-level restoration capability
through attention- and receptive-field-enhanced modules and
architecture.
To enable onboard model training, we further formulate optical cloud
removal as a decentralized onboard federated learning problem over
satellite constellations. To the best of our knowledge, this is the
first work to study this problem. To address source-level heterogeneity
in satellite observations, \OrbitALIF{} preserves local
batch-normalization statistics to mitigate feature shift and exchanges
shared parameters through intra-plane aggregation and inter-plane
Gossip mixing. Experiments show that the $2.30$\,M onboard deployment
variant improves cloud-removal quality while reducing per-image
inference energy by up to $72.3\!\times$. The scaled variant achieves
SOTA performance on image deraining and competitive near-SOTA
performance on cloud removal. These results demonstrate a new pathway
toward low-energy, decentralized, and satellite-native optical
remote-sensing restoration.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
Spiking neural networks, optical cloud removal, decentralized
federated learning, onboard satellite computing.
\end{IEEEkeywords}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Low-Earth-orbit (LEO) satellite constellations continuously acquire
large-scale optical remote-sensing imagery, but cloud contamination
degrades image usability and delays time-critical downstream tasks.
Conventional pipelines transmit raw imagery to ground servers for
processing, creating a timeliness bottleneck as contact windows are
narrow and bandwidth-constrained~\cite{ref:earthobs1,ref:onboardfl-bottleneck}.
Space Computing Power Networks (Space-CPN) propose shifting intelligent
processing onboard~\cite{ref:spacecpn1}, yet onboard cloud removal is
non-trivial: it is a high-fidelity dense regression task requiring
pixel-level recovery of cloud boundaries and fine-grained textures,
and ANN-based models incur energy costs incompatible with LEO
solar-power budgets~\cite{ref:cuhkcr}.

Spiking neural networks (SNNs) transmit information through sparse
binary events—synaptic operations fire only on spikes—enabling
order-of-magnitude energy reductions on neuromorphic hardware~\cite{ref:maass1997,ref:loihi}.
For onboard training, federated learning avoids raw-data downlink by
sharing model parameters~\cite{ref:fedavg,ref:fedbn}.
FLSNN~\cite{ref:flsnn} demonstrated decentralized federated SNN
training on a Walker-Star constellation, but only for image
\emph{classification}. Cloud removal imposes three additional gaps:
dense pixel regression rather than discrete label prediction;
multi-scale texture recovery rather than compact feature extraction;
and source-level non-IID data driven by cloud thickness and sensor
variation rather than class-label imbalance.

We propose \textbf{\OrbitALIF{}}, the first framework for
decentralized onboard federated SNN-based optical cloud removal.
Contributions:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Problem formulation.} We formulate decentralized onboard
  federated cloud removal over a $5\!\times\!10$ Walker-Star
  constellation with source-level Dirichlet heterogeneity.
\item \textbf{\OrbitALIF{} backbone.} Five-level quantized spikes
  (\FQS{}), dual-frequency residual blocks (\DFRB{}), frequency-spatial
  attention (\SHAM{}), adaptive gated fusion (\AGFM{}), and
  spectro-temporal hyper-blocks (\SSHB{}) deliver high-fidelity spiking
  cloud removal under sparse event-driven computation.
\item \textbf{System results.} The $2.30$\,M onboard variant improves
  centralized cloud removal by $+0.50$\,dB and reduces inference energy
  up to $72.3\!\times$ over a compact ANN baseline. Under
  decentralized \textsc{FedBN}\,+\,Gossip the \OrbitALIF{}-ANN
  variant exceeds PlainUNet by $+0.667$\,dB, while
  \OrbitALIF{}-SNN remains within $0.13$\,dB and is robust to
  $10\!\times$ stronger source heterogeneity.
\end{itemize}

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows.
Section~\ref{sec:sysmodel} presents the system model.
Section~\ref{sec:framework} details the \OrbitALIF{} framework.
Section~\ref{sec:experiments} reports experimental results.
Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

\section{System Model}\label{sec:sysmodel}

\subsection{Constellation and Problem Setup}\label{sec:sysmodel-setup}

We consider a Walker-Star LEO constellation of $N\!=\!5$ orbital
planes, each with $K\!=\!10$ satellites ($NK\!=\!50$ total), connected
by intra-plane (quasi-stationary) and inter-plane (chain) ISLs.
Each satellite $s_{i,k}$ holds local paired cloudy/clear image
tiles $\mathcal{D}_{i,k}$ and participates in training without
uploading raw observations. The global objective is to minimise
\begin{equation}
\min_{x \in \mathbb{R}^d}
f(x)
\;=\;
\frac{1}{NK}
\sum_{i,k}
\frac{1}{|\mathcal{D}_{i,k}|}
\!\sum_{(c,y)\in\mathcal{D}_{i,k}}\!
\ell\!\left(g_x(c),y\right),
\label{eq:globalobj}
\end{equation}
where $g_x$ is \OrbitALIF{} and $\ell$ is defined in
Section~\ref{sec:framework-loss}.

To model satellite data heterogeneity, we use a source-level
Dirichlet non-IID partition with $S\!=\!2$ source domains (CUHK-CR1:
thin cloud; CUHK-CR2: thick cloud). Each satellite draws a source
mixing vector $\mathbf{p}_{i,k}\!\sim\!\Dir(\alpha\mathbf{1}_S)$
and samples tiles accordingly. Under $\alpha\!=\!0.1$, 30 of 50
satellites hold only five tiles each, while three satellites hold
$\{117,118,118\}$ samples.

\subsection{Decentralized Training Protocol}\label{sec:sysmodel-procedure}

Training runs for $R\!=\!200$ rounds; each round has four steps.
(S1)~Each satellite performs $E\!=\!2$ local AdamW steps on
$\mathcal{D}_{i,k}$.
(S2)~Intra-plane ring AllReduce averages non-BN parameters:
\begin{equation}
x_i^{(r+\frac{1}{2})}
\;=\;
\frac{1}{K}
\sum_{k \in \mathcal{K}_i}
x_{i,k}^{(r,E)}.
\label{eq:intraplane}
\end{equation}
(S3)~Single-step inter-plane Gossip over the chain topology:
\begin{equation}
x_i^{(r+1)}
\;=\;
\sum_{j \in \mathcal{N}}
W_{ij}\, x_j^{(r+\frac{1}{2})},
\quad i \in \mathcal{N},
\label{eq:gossip}
\end{equation}
where $W$ is the symmetric doubly-stochastic chain mixing matrix.
(S4)~Intra-plane broadcast of $x_i^{(r+1)}$ to all satellites in plane $i$.

Following \textsc{FedBN}~\cite{ref:fedbn}, all BN-tagged
parameters---affine $(\gamma,\beta)$, running statistics, and TDBN
parameters---are excluded from both \eqref{eq:intraplane} and
\eqref{eq:gossip}. Only non-BN convolutional and attention weights
are exchanged, so each satellite's normalization adapts to its local
cloud-density distribution without cross-satellite interference.

\section{The \OrbitALIF{} Framework}\label{sec:framework}

\OrbitALIF{} couples an attention-enhanced LIF restoration backbone
with the decentralized constellation training pipeline of
Section~\ref{sec:sysmodel} (Figure~\ref{fig:overview}). The backbone
takes a cloudy RGB tile $\mathbf{c}\in\mathbb{R}^{3\times H\times W}$
and predicts a cloud-free image via a global residual:

\begin{equation}
\hat{\mathbf{y}}
=
\mathbf{c}
+
\mathrm{Conv}\!\left(
\overline{\mathrm{Synth}(\mathrm{Anal}(\mathbf{c}))}^{(T)}
\right),
\label{eq:globalresidual}
\end{equation}
where $T\!=\!4$ spike steps and $\overline{(\cdot)}^{(T)}$ is temporal
averaging. The onboard deployment variant uses base channel width
$C\!=\!24$ (${\approx}\,2.30$\,M parameters).

\begin{figure*}[!htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{fig1.pdf}

  pipeline: three-level multi-scale spiking backbone with full-resolution
  \SSHB{}, dual-frequency \DFRB{}, adaptive gated \AGFM{}, and
  frequency-spatial \SHAM{}; global residual adds the predicted
  correction to the cloudy input. \textbf{(b)}~Decentralized
  $5\!\times\!10$ Walker-Star constellation: intra-plane ring
  AllReduce and inter-plane Gossip mix non-BN parameters; BN-tagged
  parameters remain local.}
  \label{fig:overview}
\end{figure*}

Five components are detailed below.

\subsection{\FQS{}: Five-Level Quantized Spike}
\label{sec:framework-5qs}

Binary spikes restrict each step to a one-bit state, insufficient for
dense pixel restoration. \FQS{} clamps the membrane potential to
$[0,4]$, rounds, and normalises by 4:
\begin{equation}
o_t
=
\frac{\mathrm{round}(\mathrm{clamp}(u_t,0,4))}{4}
\;\in\;
\left\{0,\,\tfrac{1}{4},\,\tfrac{1}{2},\,\tfrac{3}{4},\,1\right\}.
\label{eq:5qs-forward}
\end{equation}
Synaptic operations fire only when $o_t\!>\!0$, preserving
event-driven sparsity with five amplitude levels.
Training uses the straight-through surrogate
$\partial o_t/\partial u_t \approx \tfrac{1}{4}\cdot
\mathbb{1}\{0\le u_t\le 4\}$.
Ablation B3 (Section~\ref{sec:exp-ablation}) confirms \FQS{}
raises PSNR by $+0.302$\,dB over binary spikes.

\subsection{\DFRB{}: Dual-Frequency Residual Block}
\label{sec:framework-dfrb}

Cloud removal needs both temporal LIF responses (cloud boundaries)
and spatial high-frequency components (fine textures). \DFRB{}
captures these through two \emph{cascaded} groups. Group~1 applies
a LIF neuron to decompose $\mathbf{X}$ into high-frequency
$\mathbf{X}_h$ and residual $\mathbf{X}_l$, then combines them with
learnable scales and a nonlinear interaction:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{Z}_1
=
\mathrm{BN}(\mathrm{Conv}(
\alpha_h\mathbf{X}_h + \alpha_l\mathbf{X}_l
+ \mathbf{X}\odot\mathbf{X}_h)).
\label{eq:dfrb-g1}
\end{equation}
Group~2 applies a \texttt{PixelShuffleLIFBlock} to extract spatial
high-frequency features $\mathbf{Z}_{2h}$ from $\mathbf{Z}_1$ and
fuses them via a learnable cross-scale gate $\sigma(g)$ initialized
to zero. After shortcut addition and TCS-Att, \SHAM{} is applied:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{DFRB}(\mathbf{X})
=
\SHAM{}\!\left(
\mathrm{TCS\text{-}Att}(\mathbf{Z}_2 + \mathrm{Conv}(\mathbf{X}))
\right).
\label{eq:dfrb}
\end{equation}
Ablation B2 shows removing the dual-group structure drops PSNR by
$-0.516$\,dB (Section~\ref{sec:exp-ablation}).

\subsection{\SHAM{}: Spectral-Spatial Hybrid Attention}
\label{sec:framework-sham}

\SHAM{} recalibrates spike features along two orthogonal axes.
Given $\mathbf{X}\in\mathbb{R}^{T\times C\times H\times W}$, the
temporal-amplitude attention (TAA) branch modulates each time step
via sigmoid-gated FC mixing over spatial-pooled descriptors:
$\mathbf{X}' = \mathbf{X} + \mathbf{X}\odot
\sigma(\mathrm{FC}_{T\to T}(\overline{\mathbf{X}}_\mathrm{sp}))$.
The frequency-spatial attention (SFA) branch applies
$\mathrm{rFFT2}$ to the temporal mean of $\mathbf{X}'$, weights
spectral components via a \texttt{FreqMLP}, reconstructs via iFFT,
and generates a spatial mask $\mathbf{w}_S$ with a $7\!\times\!7$
convolution. The full output is a learnable gated residual ($\eta$
initialized to zero):
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{SHAM}(\mathbf{X})
=
\mathbf{X}
+\sigma(\eta)\!\left(\mathbf{X}'\odot\mathbf{w}_S-\mathbf{X}\right).
\label{eq:sham}
\end{equation}
\SHAM{} embeds at the tail of each \DFRB{} (after TCS-Att);
its removal drops PSNR by $-0.597$\,dB (Ablation B1).

\subsection{\AGFM{}: Adaptive Gated Fusion}
\label{sec:framework-agfm}

In thick-cloud regions shallow features are corrupted; in thin-cloud
regions they carry reliable textures. \AGFM{} generates a spatially
varying gate from the concatenated decoder feature $\mathbf{d}$ and
encoder skip $\mathbf{e}$, and fuses them as
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{f}
=
\boldsymbol{\gamma}\odot\mathbf{d}
+(1-\boldsymbol{\gamma})\odot\mathbf{e},\quad
\boldsymbol{\gamma}=\sigma\!\left(
\mathrm{Conv}_{1\times1}([\mathbf{d};\mathbf{e}]_C)\right).
\label{eq:agfm}
\end{equation}
\AGFM{} is inserted at the first and second multi-scale levels;
the deepest level uses a direct encoder--decoder connection.

\subsection{\SSHB{}: Spectro-Temporal Spike Hyper-Block}
\label{sec:framework-sshb}

\SSHB{} enhances full-resolution features through temporal expansion,
spike-convolutional refinement, and FFT-domain spatial mixing.
Starting from $\mathbf{X}$, \DFRB{} extracts dual-frequency responses;
a $2\!\times$ PixelUnshuffle (PU$_2$) expands the time horizon from
$T$ to $T\!\cdot\!r^2\!=\!16$ steps at half resolution; a LIF node
and temporal-channel attention module (TCAM) recalibrate the expanded
representation; a 3D convolution compresses back to $T$ steps; two
spike-convolutional Refine blocks follow; the result is upsampled and
skip-added, then passed through TCS-Att and an FFT-domain FreqMLP
(FSE), yielding global receptive field in $O(N\log N)$:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{SSHB}(\mathbf{X})
=
\mathrm{FSE}\circ\mathrm{TCS\text{-}Att}\circ
(\cdot{+}\mathrm{DFRB}(\mathbf{X}))
\circ\mathrm{Refine}\circ\mathrm{TCAM}\circ\mathrm{PU}_{2}\circ\mathrm{DFRB}(\mathbf{X}).
\label{eq:sshb}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Training Objective}
\label{sec:framework-loss}

\OrbitALIF{} is trained with a composite Charbonnier\,+\,SSIM loss:
\begin{equation}
\ell(\hat{\mathbf{y}},\mathbf{y})
=
\frac{1}{|\Omega|}\!\sum_{p\in\Omega}
\sqrt{(\hat{\mathbf{y}}_p-\mathbf{y}_p)^2+\varepsilon^2}
+\lambda\!\left(1-\mathrm{SSIM}(\hat{\mathbf{y}},\mathbf{y})\right),
\label{eq:loss}
\end{equation}
with $\varepsilon\!=\!10^{-3}$ and $\lambda\!=\!0.1$. Charbonnier
handles cloud-occlusion outliers robustly; SSIM preserves structural
consistency and cloud-boundary sharpness. The constellation training
pipeline follows Section~\ref{sec:sysmodel}: all BN-tagged parameters
are kept local; only non-BN weights are exchanged.
\textsc{FedBN}~\cite{ref:fedbn} and Gossip~\cite{ref:gossip-boyd}
are adopted as system-level strategies suited to the constellation
topology; they are not proposed as new FL algorithms.

\section{Experiments}\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}\label{sec:exp-setup}

\textbf{Datasets.} CUHK-CR~\cite{ref:cuhkcr} provides paired
cloudy/clear Sentinel-2 RGB tiles. CR1 (thin cloud) and CR2 (thick
cloud) define the two Dirichlet source domains. All runs use
$64\!\times\!64$ random crops for training and centre-patch evaluation.

\textbf{Models.} Three matched-protocol backbones share the same
aggregation pipeline. \OrbitALIF{}-SNN: \FQS{}-LIF, TDBN, $T\!=\!4$,
$C\!=\!24$ ($2.30$\,M). \OrbitALIF{}-ANN: identical dataflow with
ReLU\,+\,BN in place of \FQS{}-LIF\,+\,TDBN. PlainUNet: vanilla
U-Net ($1.31$\,M) ablation baseline.

\textbf{Training.} Centralized: $600$ epochs, AdamW lr\,$10^{-3}$
cosine$\to$\,$10^{-7}$, $3$-epoch warmup, batch~$4$, gradient clip
$\|g\|_2\!\le\!1$. Ablations: $300$ epochs. Federated: $R\!=\!200$
rounds, $E\!=\!2$ local epochs, $\alpha\!=\!0.1$. Metrics: PSNR,
SSIM, cumulative inter-plane comm.\ (MB), wall time (h). Energy
analysis uses forward hooks on $32$ CR1 test patches.

\subsection{Centralized Cloud-Removal Performance}\label{sec:exp-centralized}

Table~\ref{tab:centralized} reports centralized results on CUHK-CR1
and CUHK-CR2. \OrbitALIF{} attains
$25.374$\,dB\,/\,$0.7728$ PSNR\,/\,SSIM on CR1 and
$23.197$\,dB\,/\,$0.7449$ on CR2, exceeding the compact PlainUNet
baseline by $+0.502$\,dB on CR1 and $+0.157$\,dB on CR2 despite
operating with sparse spike activations and TDBN-quantized
intermediate features. The smaller margin on CR2 reflects the harder
restoration ceiling under thick-cloud occlusion, where the encoder
features themselves carry less recoverable information.

\subsection{Component Ablation}\label{sec:exp-ablation}

We disable one \OrbitALIF{} component at a time and retrain on
CUHK-CR1 for $300$ epochs. Ablation B1 surgically replaces every
\SHAM{} block with the identity mapping; B2 replaces every \DFRB{}
with a single-group residual block that retains only the temporal
LIF group (and the shortcut, TCS-Att, and \SHAM{} tail) so that the
PixelShuffle-LIF spatial high-frequency path and the cross-scale gate
$\sigma(g)$ are removed; B3 replaces \FQS{} with a binary spike whose
forward output is $\{0,1\}$ but whose surrogate gradient window
$[0,4]$ and $1/4$ scale are kept identical to \FQS{}, so that only
the forward quantization granularity changes. \AGFM{} is not
separately ablated because its sigmoid gate degenerates to plain
addition at $\boldsymbol{\gamma}\!=\!\tfrac{1}{2}$ and no surgery is
needed in code.

\textbf{Component ranking.}\;\;Table~\ref{tab:ablation} attributes the
largest single-component PSNR drop to \SHAM{} ($-0.597$\,dB),
followed by the dual-group \DFRB{} ($-0.516$\,dB) and \FQS{}
($-0.302$\,dB). The ranking is consistent with the design rationale
of Section~\ref{sec:framework}. \SHAM{} contributes orthogonal
recalibration along the temporal-amplitude and frequency-spatial
axes that no other component recovers. The dual-group \DFRB{}
provides the spatial high-frequency path that complements the
temporal LIF group; without it the model loses fine-texture
restoration capacity. \FQS{} contributes the smallest drop because
under the $300$-epoch budget the membrane potential takes
$\sim\!50$ epochs to warm up past $V_{\rm th}$ under TDBN
initialisation, leaving the multi-level dynamic range partially
under-utilised; under the full $600$-epoch schedule of the A1 run
this gap is expected to widen.

\textbf{Co-design evidence.}\;\;A striking observation is that two of
the three single-component ablations (B1 at $24.777$\,dB and B2 at
$24.858$\,dB) fall \emph{below} the PlainUNet baseline at
$24.872$\,dB, even though the ablated networks retain the rest of the
\OrbitALIF{} architecture. Removing one component therefore breaks
\OrbitALIF{} more than reverting entirely to a plain ANN. This is
strong evidence that the \OrbitALIF{} blocks are co-designed: the
$+0.50$\,dB CR1 advantage is not the sum of independently-additive
component gains but a synergistic effect that requires all
components to operate together. Only B3, which keeps the
architecture intact and only relaxes the spike granularity, remains
above PlainUNet, confirming that \FQS{} is a refinement rather than
a load-bearing component.

\subsection{Decentralized Federated Cloud-Removal}\label{sec:exp-federated}

work: the decentralized \textsc{FedBN}\,+\,Gossip training pipeline of
Section~\ref{sec:sysmodel-procedure}. We
deploy three matched-protocol backbones on the
$5\!\times\!10\!=\!50$-satellite Walker-Star constellation under
source-level Dirichlet $\alpha\!=\!0.1$ for $R\!=\!200$ rounds.
\OrbitALIF{}-SNN is the full spiking variant; \OrbitALIF{}-ANN replaces
every \FQS{}-LIF with ReLU; PlainUNet is the compact ANN U-Net
baseline. All three share the identical aggregation pipeline of
Section~\ref{sec:sysmodel-procedure} and differ only in the
per-satellite network, so any performance gap is attributable to the
backbone alone.

\textbf{Architecture transfers to the federated setting.}\;\;Under
identical \textsc{FedBN}\,+\,Gossip, $R\!=\!200$, $\alpha\!=\!0.1$,
\OrbitALIF{}-ANN attains $22.679$\,dB\,/\,$0.6966$, exceeding the
PlainUNet floor of $22.012$\,dB by $+0.667$\,dB
($+0.0147$ SSIM)---a margin even larger than the $+0.502$\,dB CR1
margin in the centralized regime. The \OrbitALIF{} architecture
therefore retains its quality advantage when training is fully
decentralized.

\textbf{Spiking trade-off.}\;\;Replacing \FQS{}-LIF with ReLU\,+\,BN
costs \OrbitALIF{}-SNN $0.793$\,dB versus the ANN variant
($21.886$ vs $22.679$); SSIM $0.6725$ vs $0.6966$. The SNN run lands
within $0.13$\,dB of the plain ANN PlainUNet baseline despite
sparse spike activations and TDBN-quantized intermediates, and
recoups its quality loss in inference energy
(Section~\ref{sec:exp-energy}: $1.21\!\times$ to $72.3\!\times$
reduction over the ANN baseline).

\textbf{Heterogeneity sensitivity.}\;\;Tightening source heterogeneity
from $\alpha\!=\!0.1$ to $\alpha\!=\!0.01$ degrades \OrbitALIF{}-SNN
by only $-0.126$\,dB ($21.886\!\to\!21.760$,
$0.6725\!\to\!0.6691$ SSIM), confirming that \textsc{FedBN}-local
normalization absorbs most of the additional source skew without
re-tuning the protocol. The remaining $2.86$\,dB centralized-vs-federated
gap on PlainUNet (CR1, $24.872\!\to\!22.012$) reflects the difficulty
of the $\alpha\!=\!0.1$ partition itself: $30/50$ satellites hold
only five tiles each, so single satellites cannot reconstruct the
full encoder representation under $R\!=\!200$ Gossip rounds.

\subsection{Energy Analysis}\label{sec:exp-energy}

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{fig6_energy_bars.pdf}
  \caption{Per-image estimated inference energy on a
  $64\!\times\!64$ CUHK-CR1 centre patch. ANN baseline at
  $4.6$\,pJ/MAC (Horowitz, ISSCC 2014, $45$\,nm CMOS); \OrbitALIF{}
  is reported as a dual bound: a \emph{conservative upper bound}
  that charges every non-zero MAC at the full ANN rate, and a
  \emph{deployment-target lower bound} at $77$\,fJ/SOP for
  neuromorphic substrates. Multipliers vs.\ ANN are annotated above
  each bar.}
  \label{fig:energy}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Methodology and disclosure.}\;\;Per-image inference energy is
\emph{estimated}, not hardware-measured. We hook every
\texttt{Conv2d}/\texttt{Linear} layer to count MACs and the input
non-zero rate $r_\ell$, and every LIF / \FQS{} module to record the
output firing rate, on $32$ CUHK-CR1 test patches at
$64\!\times\!64$. The MAC counts and per-layer rates are real
measurements; the per-operation energy constants are taken from the
literature. We use $4.6$\,pJ/MAC for the ANN baseline (Horowitz, ISSCC
2014, $45$\,nm CMOS) and $77$\,fJ/SOP for the SNN deployment estimate,
the constant adopted by FLSNN~\cite{ref:flsnn} and
ESDNet~\cite{ref:esdnet} for neuromorphic substrates such as
Loihi-2~\cite{ref:loihi}. Hardware-measured power on actual
neuromorphic platforms is deferred to deployment validation.

\textbf{Total energy bounds.}\;\;Figure~\ref{fig:energy} reports
per-image bounds. The ANN baseline at $4.6$\,pJ/MAC over the
$4.51$\,GMAC inference consumes $20.75$\,mJ. \OrbitALIF{} is reported
as a dual bound. The \emph{conservative upper bound} of $17.14$\,mJ
($1.21\!\times\!$ reduction) charges every non-zero MAC at the full
$4.6$\,pJ ANN rate and is achievable without any neuromorphic
hardware. The \emph{deployment-target lower bound} of
$0.287$\,mJ ($72.3\!\times\!$ reduction) uses $77$\,fJ/SOP and
applies to substrates that support the full SOP / spike abstraction
(e.g., Loihi-2, Speck, Akida). Across CR1 and CR2 inferences on the
same architecture, the measured effective non-zero MAC ratios are
$\bar{r}_{\rm CR1}\!=\!0.826$ and $\bar{r}_{\rm CR2}\!=\!0.823$,
yielding indistinguishable energy bounds at two decimal places. We
do not claim data-independence of the energy estimate; the two
CUHK-CR distributions are simply close enough to give consistent
bounds.

\textbf{Per-layer firing rates.}\;\;Forward hooks on all LIF and \FQS{}
modules measure $20$\,--\,$70\,\%$ non-zero activation across the
analysis path; \FQS{} layers show a higher non-zero floor because
four of five quantization levels are non-zero by construction. These
per-layer rates $r_\ell$ directly enter the energy formula
$E_{\rm SNN}\!=\!\sum_\ell\mathrm{MAC}_\ell\,r_\ell\,e_{\rm op}$.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented \OrbitALIF{}, the first end-to-end decentralized
federated learning framework for satellite-onboard SNN-based optical
cloud removal. The framework couples an attention-enhanced LIF
restoration backbone with a constellation-native training pipeline.
The backbone integrates five-level quantized spikes (\FQS{}),
dual-frequency residual blocks (\DFRB{}), spectral-spatial hybrid
attention (\SHAM{}), adaptive gated cross-stage fusion (\AGFM{}),
and full-resolution spectro-temporal hyper-blocks (\SSHB{}) into a
sparse spiking dataflow that preserves pixel-level restoration
capability. The training pipeline runs entirely between satellites
through intra-plane ring AllReduce and inter-plane Gossip mixing,
with all BN-tagged parameters kept local under the \textsc{FedBN}
protocol; this avoids both the bandwidth cost of downlinking raw
observations and the dependence on a centralized ground parameter
server.

Empirically, the $2.30$\,M onboard deployment variant attains
$25.37$\,dB\,/\,$0.7728$ PSNR\,/\,SSIM on CUHK-CR1 and
$23.20$\,dB\,/\,$0.7449$ on CUHK-CR2, exceeding a compact PlainUNet
baseline by $+0.50$\,dB on CR1 and $+0.16$\,dB on CR2. Per-image
inference energy on a $64\!\times\!64$ patch is estimated as
$1.21\!\times$ to $72.3\!\times$ lower than the ANN baseline of
$20.75$\,mJ under standard literature pJ-per-operation constants.
Component ablations show the building blocks form a co-designed
whole: removing \SHAM{} or the dual-group \DFRB{} drops PSNR
\emph{below} the plain ANN baseline, while removing \FQS{} (binary
spikes) keeps performance above PlainUNet. Under the
\textsc{FedBN}\,+\,Gossip protocol on the $5\!\times\!10$ Walker-Star
constellation with source-level Dirichlet $\alpha\!=\!0.1$,
\OrbitALIF{}-ANN reaches $22.679$\,dB ($+0.667$\,dB over PlainUNet),
showing the architectural advantage transfers to the decentralized
setting; \OrbitALIF{}-SNN reaches $21.886$\,dB, within $0.13$\,dB of
the plain ANN federated baseline and degrading by only $-0.126$\,dB
under $10\!\times$ stronger heterogeneity ($\alpha\!=\!0.01$).

Future directions include: (i) closing the federated-vs-centralized
gap via partial participation, momentum aggregation, or
orbital-geometry-aware mixing matrices; and (ii) hardware validation
on neuromorphic substrates (Loihi-2, Speck, Akida) to replace the
analytic energy bracket with measured on-orbit power draw.
