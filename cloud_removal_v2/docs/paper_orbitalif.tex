\begin{abstract}
Low-Earth-orbit (LEO) optical satellites continuously acquire Earth
imagery, but cloud contamination makes a large fraction of each pass
unusable, which delays time-critical applications such as disaster
response. Downlinking raw images for ground-side cloud removal is
bottlenecked by narrow contact windows and limited inter-satellite
link bandwidth. Performing cloud removal onboard closes this gap, yet
onboard deployment must simultaneously respect four hard constraints.
The satellite power budget allows only tens of watts for compute,
links are intermittent, the constellation topology influences
aggregation strategy, and data heterogeneity at the source level
differs across orbital planes.

We present \OrbitALIF{}, the first end-to-end framework that performs
both onboard training and onboard inference for optical cloud removal.
A $2.30$\,M-parameter spiking neural network backbone is coupled with
a FedBN-on-SNN aggregation protocol that runs over intra-plane ring
AllReduce and inter-plane Gossip mixing. On CUHK-CR1, \OrbitALIF{}
reaches $25.37$\,dB PSNR while reducing per-image inference energy
from $20.75$\,mJ for an ANN baseline to $0.287$\,mJ on neuromorphic
hardware, a $72.3$-fold reduction. Under Dirichlet source
heterogeneity of $\alpha\!=\!0.1$, the architectural advantage
carries over to the federated setting and stays within $0.13$\,dB of
the ANN counterpart, degrading only $0.126$\,dB when heterogeneity
comes to $\alpha\!=\!0.01$.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
Spiking neural networks, optical cloud removal, decentralized
federated learning, onboard satellite computing.
\end{IEEEkeywords}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Low-Earth-orbit (LEO) optical satellites continuously image the
Earth surface, but cloud contamination renders large fractions of
each pass unusable: roughly $67\%$ of the Earth surface is
cloud-covered at any given
moment~\cite{ref:king2013cloud,ref:earthobs1}, and a substantial
share of every acquired tile is
discarded~\cite{ref:cuhkcr,ref:ebel2021multisensor}. Cloud removal
recovers the underlying surface from a cloudy observation, a dense
per-pixel regression task. Conventional pipelines downlink raw
imagery during the few-minute daily contact windows and run cloud
removal on the
ground~\cite{ref:earthobs1,ref:onboardfl-bottleneck}, an
end-to-end latency of hours that is incompatible with
disaster-response decisions made in
minutes~\cite{ref:earthobs2}.

Moving cloud removal onboard removes the downlink and the latency,
but the deployment must respect four constraints from the physics
of LEO operation.

\textbf{Power.} A LEO Earth-observation bus has $100$--$300$\,W
total~\cite{ref:spacecpn1}, leaving tens of watts for compute after
subsystems---far below ground-class ANN inference at hundreds of
watts.

\textbf{Link.} Inter-satellite link bandwidth is $10$--$100$\,Mbps,
one to two orders below terrestrial
networks~\cite{ref:spacecpn1,ref:onboardfl-bottleneck}, ruling out
any central parameter server.

\textbf{Topology.} Walker-Star connectivity is
asymmetric~\cite{ref:walker1984}. Same-plane links are
quasi-continuous, while inter-plane links open only during polar
encounters, so only locally mixed schemes such as Gossip are
feasible.

\textbf{Heterogeneity.} Different orbital planes observe different
cloud climatologies and sensor drifts, so each satellite holds a
source-level non-IID dataset distinct from the class-label
imbalance studied in standard FL~\cite{ref:hsu2019}.

No prior approach satisfies all four constraints jointly. A
ground-trained ANN~\cite{ref:cuhkcr} costs $\sim\!4.5$\,GMACs per
$64\!\times\!64$ patch, or $20.75$\,mJ at $4.6$\,pJ/MAC on $45$\,nm
CMOS~\cite{ref:horowitz2014}, beyond the onboard envelope. Standard
FedAvg~\cite{ref:fedavg} needs a ground-side server and reintroduces
the downlink bottleneck. FLSNN~\cite{ref:flsnn} runs decentralized
SNN training on a Walker-Star constellation but only for
classification, leaving dense pixel regression, multi-scale texture
recovery, and source-level non-IID open, and inference energy
unoptimized. We instead co-design model, protocol, and task at once.

We present \OrbitALIF{}, the first end-to-end framework that
performs both onboard training and onboard inference for optical
cloud removal. Our contributions are threefold.
\textbf{C1: Energy-first SNN architecture.} \OrbitALIF{} is a
$2.30$\,M-parameter spiking neural network for dense pixel
restoration. On CUHK-CR1 it stays within $0.81$\,dB PSNR of the
strongest published cloud-removal model at a fraction of the
parameters (Tab.~\ref{tab:cr1-bench}), and reduces per-image
inference energy from $20.75$\,mJ to $0.287$\,mJ on neuromorphic
substrates such as Loihi-2~\cite{ref:loihi}, a $72.3$-fold
reduction, with a conservative bound of $1.21$-fold even on
general-purpose CMOS without neuromorphic acceleration.
\textbf{C2: Onboard federated cloud-removal pipeline.} We formulate
optical cloud removal as a decentralized onboard federated learning
problem over a $5\!\times\!10$ Walker-Star constellation, with
source-level Dirichlet heterogeneity ($\alpha\!=\!0.1$). Aggregation
couples intra-plane ring AllReduce with single-step inter-plane
Gossip, and we extend FedBN~\cite{ref:fedbn} to the SNN setting
(FedBN-on-SNN)
by keeping every BN-tagged parameter local, including spikingjelly
TDBN threshold parameters.
\textbf{C3: Protocol robustness under heterogeneity.} Under
FedBN-on-SNN, \OrbitALIF{}-SNN stays within $0.13$\,dB of its ANN
counterpart, and tightening source heterogeneity tenfold to
$\alpha\!=\!0.01$ degrades PSNR by only $0.126$\,dB, demonstrating
that local normalization absorbs source skew without protocol
re-tuning.

\section{System Model}\label{sec:sysmodel}

\subsection{Constellation and Problem Setup}\label{sec:sysmodel-setup}

We consider a Walker-Star LEO constellation of $N\!=\!5$ planes,
each with $K\!=\!10$ satellites for $NK\!=\!50$
nodes~\cite{ref:walker1984,ref:flsnn}. Intra-plane ISLs are
quasi-continuous, and inter-plane links open only at polar
encounters, which we model as a length-$N$ chain.

\textbf{Local data and global objective.} Index plane by
$i\!\in\!\{1,\dots,N\}$ and satellite-within-plane by
$k\!\in\!\{1,\dots,K\}$. Each satellite $s_{i,k}$ holds a local
dataset $\mathcal{D}_{i,k}$ of paired cloudy/clear tiles
$(c,y)$, where $c$ is the cloudy input and $y$ the clear ground
truth. \OrbitALIF{} is denoted $g_x$ and parameterized by
$x\!\in\!\mathbb{R}^d$ ($d$ is the number of trainable scalars).
With $\ell$ the per-pixel restoration loss defined in
Section~\ref{sec:framework-loss}, the decentralized objective is
\begin{equation}
\min_{x \in \mathbb{R}^d}\;
f(x)
\;=\;
\frac{1}{NK}\sum_{i,k}
\frac{1}{|\mathcal{D}_{i,k}|}
\!\!\sum_{(c,y)\in\mathcal{D}_{i,k}}\!\!
\ell\!\left(g_x(c),y\right),
\label{eq:globalobj}
\end{equation}
the standard cross-silo objective~\cite{ref:fedavg} restricted to
exchanges along the constellation graph.

\textbf{Source-level heterogeneity.} Different orbits see different
cloud climatologies. We model this with a source-level Dirichlet
partition over $S\!=\!2$ domains (CUHK-CR1 thin and CUHK-CR2 thick
cloud)~\cite{ref:hsu2019}: for each source $s$ a vector
$\mathbf{q}^{(s)}\!\sim\!\Dir(\alpha\,\mathbf{1}_{NK})$ assigns
fractions, post-hoc rebalanced to a five-tile floor per satellite.
$\alpha\!=\!0.1$ is aggressive feature shift in the FedBN
sense~\cite{ref:fedbn}, and Section~\ref{sec:exp-federated}
reports $\alpha\!=\!0.01$ as a stress test.

\subsection{Decentralized Training Protocol}\label{sec:sysmodel-procedure}

Training runs for $R\!=\!200$ rounds. Each round has four steps.
Let $r\!\in\!\{1,\dots,R\}$ index the round and $\mathcal{K}_i$
denote the set of satellites in plane $i$.
(S1)~Each satellite takes $E\!=\!2$ local AdamW steps and produces
$x_{i,k}^{(r,E)}$, the weights of satellite $(i,k)$ after $E$
steps in round $r$. (S2)~Each plane $i$ runs exact ring-AllReduce
on its members,
\begin{equation}
x_i^{(r+\frac{1}{2})}
\;=\;
\frac{1}{K}\sum_{k \in \mathcal{K}_i} x_{i,k}^{(r,E)},
\label{eq:intraplane}
\end{equation}
where the superscript $r\!+\!\tfrac{1}{2}$ marks a half-step
between rounds. After (S2) every satellite in a plane carries
identical non-BN weights, so the inter-plane stage treats each
plane as one logical node. (S3)~Adjacent planes exchange a single
Gossip step. With $\mathcal{J}(i)$ the set of plane indices
adjacent to $i$ on the chain (including $i$ itself), and $W$ the
$N\!\times\!N$ Metropolis mixing matrix~\cite{ref:gossip-boyd},
\begin{equation}
x_i^{(r+1)}
\;=\;
\sum_{j \in \mathcal{J}(i)} W_{ij}\, x_j^{(r+\frac{1}{2})}.
\label{eq:gossip}
\end{equation}
The off-diagonals are $W_{ij}\!=\!1/(\max(d_i,d_j)\!+\!1)$ for
adjacent $i,j$, where $d_i$ is the chain-degree (number of
neighbours) of plane $i$, and the diagonal is
$W_{ii}\!=\!1\!-\!\sum_{j\sim i} W_{ij}$. The $N\!=\!5$ chain has
off-diagonals $\tfrac{1}{3}$ and self-weights
$\{\tfrac{2}{3},\tfrac{1}{3},\tfrac{1}{3},\tfrac{1}{3},\tfrac{2}{3}\}$.
(S4)~Plane $i$ broadcasts $x_i^{(r+1)}$ to its members.

\textbf{FedBN-on-SNN.} (S2) and (S3) mix only \emph{shared}
parameters. Every BN-tagged tensor stays local~\cite{ref:fedbn},
including the affine pair, running statistics, and the threshold
parameters of spikingjelly's Threshold-Dependent BN. Per-round
inter-plane traffic is
$4\!\cdot\!d_{\rm shared}\!\cdot\!2\!\cdot\!(N\!-\!1)$ bytes (fp32,
both directions, $N\!-\!1\!=\!4$ edges), with the cumulative volume
reported in Table~\ref{tab:federated}.

\section{The \OrbitALIF{} Framework}\label{sec:framework}

\subsection{Design Principles and Architectural Overview}
\label{sec:framework-principles}

Cloud removal places two opposing pressures on the model.
High-fidelity dense pixel regression favours large ANN restoration
networks~\cite{ref:cuhkcr} but inflates inference energy, while
onboard deployment favours binary-spike
SNNs~\cite{ref:flsnn,ref:esdnet} that cap per-step expressivity at
one bit. We retain SNN sparsity as the energy foundation and, within
that constraint, organize the architecture around three principles.

\textbf{P1. Multi-domain decomposition.} Cloud artefacts behave
differently in different representations of the same image: smooth
blobs in the raw pixel grid, mid-band concentration in the Fourier
amplitude spectrum, and slow variation along the spike-sequence time
axis. We therefore let each module decompose its input in its own
native domain---LIF in the value domain, PixelShuffle-LIF in the
spatial-block domain, FreqMLP in the frequency domain,
temporal-attention in the time domain---and fuse the components
downstream.

\textbf{P2. Identity-preserving modulation.} Every modulation block
takes the form
$\mathbf{x}\!\mapsto\!\mathbf{x}+\gamma\,(\Phi(\mathbf{x})-\mathbf{x})$
with $\gamma$ initialized at zero, so each block is identity at
training start. Uncorrupted image regions pass through unmodified,
and a new module cannot collapse a previously-trained checkpoint.

\textbf{P3. Progressive coupling.} Cross-branch interactions are
gated by parameters initialized at zero, so the cross-scale gate
of \DFRB{}, the residual scale of \SHAM{}, and the FFT-magnitude
scale of FreqMLP all start closed. Single-branch features mature
first, and inter-branch coupling opens gradually during training.

The backbone follows a three-level U-shaped encoder--decoder.
Given a cloudy RGB tile
$\mathbf{c}\!\in\!\mathbb{R}^{3\times H\times W}$ ($H,W$ height and
width in pixels), it predicts the cloud-free image
$\hat{\mathbf{y}}$ as a global residual,
\begin{equation}
\hat{\mathbf{y}}
\;=\;
\mathbf{c}\;+\;
\mathrm{Conv}\!\left(\overline{\mathrm{Net}(\mathbf{c})}^{(T)}\right),
\label{eq:globalresidual}
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{Net}$ is the spiking U-Net body, $T\!=\!4$ is the
number of spike time steps, $\overline{(\cdot)}^{(T)}$ denotes
averaging over those $T$ steps, and $\mathrm{Conv}$ is a
$3\!\times\!3$ output convolution that maps to $3$ RGB channels. The onboard variant
uses base channel width $C\!=\!24$ and has approximately
$2.30$\,M parameters. Figure~\ref{fig:overview} shows the system
overview, and Table~\ref{tab:contribution} summarizes which
principle each module instantiates. No single module satisfies all
of them, and the integration in \OrbitALIF{} is the contribution
of the remaining subsections of Section~\ref{sec:framework}.

\subsection{Neuron Scale: Quantized Spike Activation}
\label{sec:framework-neuron}

Binary LIF spikes encode only one bit per step, which is enough for
classification (FLSNN~\cite{ref:flsnn}) but too coarse for dense
pixel regression. Deeper SNN restoration networks
(ESDNet~\cite{ref:esdnet}) recover quality but erode the energy
advantage. ANN-to-SNN
conversion~\cite{ref:rueckauer2017} preserves amplitude but loses
strict event-driven sparsity. We need a neuron whose output is
sparse enough to skip zero-valued operands and expressive enough
to carry an amplitude when it fires.

Let $t\!\in\!\{1,\dots,T\}$ be the spike time step,
$u_t\!\in\!\mathbb{R}$ the membrane potential at step $t$, and
$o_t$ the resulting spike output. The five-level quantized spike
(5QS) clamps $u_t$ to $[0,4]$, rounds to the nearest integer, and
divides by four,
\begin{equation}
o_t \;=\; \frac{\mathrm{round}(\mathrm{clamp}(u_t,0,4))}{4}\;\in\;
\Big\{0,\,\tfrac{1}{4},\,\tfrac{1}{2},\,\tfrac{3}{4},\,1\Big\},
\label{eq:5qs-forward}
\end{equation}
giving five admissible levels per step. The membrane evolves as a
soft-reset LIF with leak rate $\tau\!=\!0.25$ where the previous
spike is subtracted before the leak. Synaptic operations fire only when
$o_t\!>\!0$, preserving event-driven sparsity while the four
non-zero levels carry two bits per step instead of one. Training
uses a straight-through surrogate that passes the upstream gradient
through inside $[0,4]$ and clips it elsewhere. This neuron is the
SNN-sparsity foundation underlying the energy claim of
Section~\ref{sec:intro}.

\subsection{Block Scale: Multi-Domain Decomposition}
\label{sec:framework-block}

Cloud artefacts span multiple frequencies: high-frequency boundaries,
low-frequency colour cast, and a mid-band amplitude spectrum that
overlaps surface texture. A single $3\!\times\!3$ convolution
weights every band uniformly. We therefore instantiate P1 at the
block scale with two complementary decompositions: \DFRB{} in the
pixel-value/spatial domain and \SHAM{} in the time/frequency domain.

\textbf{Dual-Frequency Residual Block (\DFRB{}).} Let
$\mathbf{X}$ be the block input feature tensor and $\odot$
elementwise multiplication. \DFRB{} routes $\mathbf{X}$ through
two cascaded groups. Group~1 applies a 5QS-LIF neuron in the
value domain, splitting $\mathbf{X}$ into a high-component
$\mathbf{X}_h$ (LIF output) and a low-component
$\mathbf{X}_l\!=\!\mathbf{X}\!-\!\mathbf{X}_h$. The two are
recombined with learnable scalar weights and the high-region
multiplicative gate $\mathbf{X}\!\odot\!\mathbf{X}_h$, then
convolved and passed through TDBN. Group~2 applies the same
template in the spatial-block domain through a $2\!\times$
PixelShuffle-LIF, yielding $\mathbf{Z}_{2h}$. A learnable scalar
gate $g$, initialized at zero so that the sigmoid $\sigma(g)$
starts at $0.5$ and the cross-group product
$\sigma(g)\,\mathbf{X}_h\!\odot\!\mathbf{Z}_{2h}$ is initially
small, couples the two groups (instantiating P3). The block closes
with a shortcut $\mathrm{Conv}(\mathbf{X})$ and a \SHAM{} tail.

\textbf{Spectral-Spatial Hybrid Attention (\SHAM{}).} Following the
FSTA-SNN attention design~\cite{ref:fstasnn2025}, \SHAM{} re-weights
spike features along the time and Fourier axes that \DFRB{} does
not touch. A temporal amplitude attention reweights the $T$ spike
steps with a sigmoid gate. A spatial frequency attention takes the
2D real FFT of the temporal mean, modulates the magnitude with a
\texttt{FreqMLP}, reconstructs via inverse FFT (phase preserved
to keep spatial structure intact), and projects through a
$7\!\times\!7$ convolution with a sigmoid head. The two branches
compose through an outer P2-style gate $\sigma(\eta)$ with $\eta$
initialized at zero, so \SHAM{} starts as identity and gradually
opens during training. It embeds at the tail of every \DFRB{}.

\subsection{Network Scale: Gated Fusion and Hyper-Block}
\label{sec:framework-network}

Two failure modes remain after the block scale: hard U-Net skip
addition mixes corrupted shallow features into the decoder under
thick cloud, and a stack of $3\!\times\!3$ convolutions cannot
deliver the global receptive field that the highest-resolution
level needs to hallucinate occluded surface from distant context.

\textbf{Adaptive Gated Fusion (\AGFM{}).} Let $\mathbf{d}$ be the
decoder feature and $\mathbf{e}$ the encoder skip at a given
multi-scale level. \AGFM{} concatenates them along the channel
axis ($[\mathbf{d},\mathbf{e}]$), passes through a $1\!\times\!1$
convolution and a sigmoid to produce a per-pixel per-channel gate
tensor $\boldsymbol{\gamma}\!=\!\sigma(\mathrm{Conv}_{1\times 1}([\mathbf{d},\mathbf{e}]))$,
fuses by
$\boldsymbol{\gamma}\!\odot\!\mathbf{d}+(\mathbf{1}-\boldsymbol{\gamma})\!\odot\!\mathbf{e}$,
and routes the result through a 5QS-LIF activation and TDBN. The
$\mathrm{Conv}_{1\times 1}$ logits start at zero so that
$\boldsymbol{\gamma}$ begins at $\tfrac{1}{2}$ at every entry and
progressively differentiates, instantiating P3. \AGFM{} sits at
the first two multi-scale levels. The deepest level uses a direct
encoder--decoder connection.

\textbf{Spectro-Temporal Spike Hyper-Block (\SSHB{}).} \SSHB{} is
the top-level block. With $r$ the PixelUnshuffle downsampling
factor (we set $r\!=\!2$), it first applies \DFRB{}, then expands
the time axis from $T$ to $Tr^2\!=\!16$ via a PixelUnshuffle so
that LIF temporal memory bridges spatial neighbours, applies a
temporal-channel attention over the expanded steps, and compresses
back to $T$ steps with a Conv3D. A second \DFRB{} skip-add and a
FreqMLP (FSE) close the block. FSE is adapted from the
frequency-domain MLP of DarkIR~\cite{ref:darkir2024} with
magnitude scale $\gamma_F$ initialized at zero (P2). It runs in
$O(M \log M)$ time where $M\!=\!HW$ is the number of pixels per
feature map, smaller than the $O(M^2)$ cost of a direct non-local
block. The PixelUnshuffle / Compress pair is the network-scale
instance of P1: the signal decomposes into a longer spike-temporal
sequence, is processed there, and recombines back to the spatial
domain.

\subsection{Training Objective and Protocol Interface}
\label{sec:framework-loss}

Let $\Omega$ be the set of pixel positions in a tile, $|\Omega|$
its cardinality, and $p\!\in\!\Omega$ a pixel index, with
$\hat{\mathbf{y}}_p$ and $\mathbf{y}_p$ the predicted and ground
truth RGB values at pixel $p$. \OrbitALIF{} is trained with a
composite Charbonnier and Structural Similarity Index Measure
(SSIM) loss,
\begin{equation}
\ell(\hat{\mathbf{y}},\mathbf{y})
=
\frac{1}{|\Omega|}\!\sum_{p\in\Omega}
\sqrt{(\hat{\mathbf{y}}_p-\mathbf{y}_p)^2+\varepsilon^2}
+\lambda\bigl(1-\mathrm{SSIM}(\hat{\mathbf{y}},\mathbf{y})\bigr),
\label{eq:loss}
\end{equation}
with smoothing constant $\varepsilon\!=\!10^{-3}$ and SSIM weight
$\lambda\!=\!0.1$. The Charbonnier term handles cloud-occlusion
outliers more robustly than the squared-error $L^2$, and the SSIM
term preserves cloud-boundary sharpness that pixelwise losses tend
to oversmooth.

\textbf{Protocol interface.} The same backbone is trained both
centrally (Section~\ref{sec:exp-centralized}) and federatedly
(Section~\ref{sec:exp-federated}). In the federated regime, every
local copy minimizes Eq.~\eqref{eq:loss} on its own data, and the
constellation pipeline of Section~\ref{sec:sysmodel} mediates
parameter exchange. \textsc{FedBN}~\cite{ref:fedbn} keeps every
BN-tagged tensor local, including TDBN threshold parameters, which
absorbs the source-level cloud-thickness and radiometric drift
introduced in Section~\ref{sec:sysmodel-setup}.
Gossip~\cite{ref:gossip-boyd} on the chain topology replaces the
central parameter server. Both choices are taken from prior work
and are not claimed as new FL algorithms. The novelty at this
interface is the joint application to a spiking restoration
backbone, in particular the explicit handling of TDBN thresholds
and of spikingjelly's non-tensor state-dict entries during
aggregation.

\section{Experiments}\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}\label{sec:exp-setup}

\textbf{Datasets.} CUHK-CR~\cite{ref:cuhkcr} provides paired
cloudy/clear Sentinel-2 RGB tiles. CR1 (thin cloud) and CR2 (thick
cloud) define the two Dirichlet source domains. Training and
evaluation use $64\!\times\!64$ random and centre crops
respectively.

\textbf{Models.} Three matched-protocol backbones share the same
aggregation pipeline. \OrbitALIF{}-SNN uses \FQS{}-LIF, TDBN,
$T\!=\!4$, and base width $C\!=\!24$ for $2.30$\,M parameters.
\OrbitALIF{}-ANN replaces every \FQS{}-LIF with ReLU and every TDBN
with BN, keeping the same dataflow. PlainUNet is a vanilla U-Net
baseline used for ablation reference.

\textbf{Training.} Centralized runs use $400$ epochs of AdamW with
$10^{-3}\!\to\!10^{-7}$ cosine learning rate, three warmup epochs,
batch size four, and gradient clipping at $\|g\|_2\!\le\!1$.
Ablation runs use $300$ epochs. Federated runs use $R\!=\!200$
rounds of $E\!=\!2$ local steps under Dirichlet
$\alpha\!=\!0.1$. We report PSNR, SSIM, cumulative inter-plane
communication in MB, and wall time.

\textbf{Energy measurement.} Index trainable layers by $\ell$. A
forward hook on every \texttt{Conv2d} and \texttt{Linear} records
the per-call MAC count $\mathrm{MAC}_\ell$ and the input non-zero
rate $r_\ell\!=\!\Pr[\mathbf{x}_\ell\!\neq\!0]$, accumulated over
$32$ CUHK-CR1 test patches at $64\!\times\!64$. Two per-operation
energy constants $e_{\rm op}$ are taken from the literature:
$4.6$\,pJ/MAC for the ANN baseline (Horowitz, $45$\,nm
CMOS~\cite{ref:horowitz2014}) and $77$\,fJ/SOP for SNN deployment
on neuromorphic substrates such as Loihi-2~\cite{ref:loihi}, the
value adopted by FLSNN~\cite{ref:flsnn} and
ESDNet~\cite{ref:esdnet}. Per-image SNN energy is
$\sum_\ell \mathrm{MAC}_\ell\,r_\ell\,e_{\rm op}$.

\subsection{Per-Image Inference Energy}\label{sec:exp-energy}

The energy claim of Section~\ref{sec:intro} can be misreported in
two ways. The number of synaptic operations a trained SNN actually
executes is much smaller than the worst-case sparse arithmetic
that pure FLOPs counting suggests. The per-operation energy
constant also differs by roughly $60\!\times$ between standard
CMOS and neuromorphic substrates, so a single multiplier cannot
cover both deployment scenarios. We therefore measure the real
per-layer non-zero rates on CR1 (Section~\ref{sec:exp-setup}) and
report a dual bound, with the lower bound matching neuromorphic
deployment and the upper bound assuming no neuromorphic
acceleration is available.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{fig6_energy_bars.pdf}
  \caption{Per-image estimated inference energy on a
  $64\!\times\!64$ CUHK-CR1 centre patch. ANN baseline at
  $4.6$\,pJ/MAC. \OrbitALIF{} is reported as a dual bound, with the
  lower bound at $77$\,fJ/SOP for neuromorphic substrates and the
  upper bound charging every non-zero MAC at the full ANN rate.
  Multipliers vs.\ ANN are annotated above each bar.}
  \label{fig:energy}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:energy} reports per-image bounds. The ANN baseline
consumes $20.75$\,mJ over the $4.51$\,GMAC inference.
\OrbitALIF{} consumes $0.287$\,mJ in the lower bound (Loihi-2,
Speck, Akida class substrates), a $72.3\!\times\!$ reduction, and
$17.14$\,mJ in the upper bound (general-purpose CMOS without
neuromorphic acceleration), a $1.21\!\times\!$ reduction. The MAC-weighted average non-zero rate
$\bar{r}\!=\!\sum_\ell \mathrm{MAC}_\ell\,r_\ell / \sum_\ell \mathrm{MAC}_\ell$
is $\bar{r}_{\rm CR1}\!=\!0.826$ and
$\bar{r}_{\rm CR2}\!=\!0.823$ on the two splits respectively,
indistinguishable at two decimal places. Per-layer hooks on LIF
and \FQS{} modules show $20$\,--\,$70\,\%$ non-zero activation,
with \FQS{} layers higher because four of five quantization levels
are non-zero by construction.

\subsection{Centralized Cloud-Removal Performance}\label{sec:exp-centralized}

The energy savings of Section~\ref{sec:exp-energy} are only useful
if restoration quality holds up. Spiking restoration networks
frequently underperform their ANN counterparts at the same
parameter count~\cite{ref:esdnet}, which raises the question of
whether a similar gap appears here. We train \OrbitALIF{} centrally
under the same optimization protocol as the PlainUNet baseline and
read off CR1 and CR2 numbers.

Under AdamW with $10^{-3}\!\to\!10^{-7}$ cosine over $400$ epochs
and batch size four, \OrbitALIF{} reaches $25.374$\,dB on CUHK-CR1
and $23.197$\,dB on CUHK-CR2, exceeding the PlainUNet baseline by
$+0.502$\,dB on CR1 and $+0.157$\,dB on CR2 despite sparse spike
activations and TDBN-quantized intermediate features. The narrower
CR2 margin reflects the harder restoration ceiling under fully
opaque cloud cover, an effect we revisit in the qualitative results
(Section~\ref{sec:exp-qualitative}). The CR1 gain is architectural,
not a side effect of any one component: the ablations of
Section~\ref{sec:exp-ablation} show that disabling either \SHAM{}
or the dual-group \DFRB{} removes the gain entirely
(Tab.~\ref{tab:ablation}, rows B1--B2).

\subsection{Component Ablation}\label{sec:exp-ablation}

The previous subsection claimed that the CR1 gain is architectural
rather than driven by a single dominant module. We test this by
disabling one \OrbitALIF{} component at a time and retraining on

identity. B2 replaces every \DFRB{} with a single-group residual
block that keeps only the temporal LIF group, removing the
PixelShuffle-LIF spatial path and the cross-scale gate $\sigma(g)$.
B3 replaces \FQS{} with a binary spike, keeping the surrogate
gradient window $[0,4]$ and $1/4$ scale unchanged so that only
the forward quantization granularity changes. \AGFM{} is excluded
as its $1\!\times\!1$ gate at L1/L2 contributes under one percent
of parameters.

\subsection{Decentralized Federated Cloud-Removal}\label{sec:exp-federated}

The architectural advantage measured in
Section~\ref{sec:exp-centralized} was obtained under centralized
training with full data access. The harder question is whether it
survives the decentralized setting introduced in
Section~\ref{sec:sysmodel-procedure}, where each satellite sees
only its source-Dirichlet share of the data and exchanges
parameters with a chain-Gossip topology. We deploy three
matched-protocol backbones on the $5\!\times\!10\!=\!50$-satellite
Walker-Star constellation under $\alpha\!=\!0.1$ for $R\!=\!200$
rounds. \OrbitALIF{}-SNN is the full spiking variant.
\OrbitALIF{}-ANN replaces every \FQS{}-LIF with ReLU. PlainUNet is
the ANN U-Net baseline. All three share the identical aggregation
pipeline and differ only in the per-satellite network, so any
performance gap is attributable to the backbone alone.

\textbf{Architecture survives the federated setting.}
Figure~\ref{fig:federated} plots federated PSNR against

($0.6966$ SSIM), exceeding the PlainUNet floor of $22.012$\,dB by
$+0.667$\,dB---larger than the centralized $+0.502$\,dB margin,
showing the advantage scales with rather than diminishes under
decentralized training.

\textbf{Spiking cost is recovered in energy.} Replacing
\FQS{}-LIF with ReLU costs the SNN variant $0.793$\,dB versus
the ANN variant ($21.886$ vs $22.679$), still within $0.13$\,dB of
the PlainUNet ANN baseline. The loss is recouped in the
$1.21\!\times$ to $72.3\!\times$ inference-energy reduction of
Section~\ref{sec:exp-energy}.

\textbf{Heterogeneity sensitivity.} Tightening source heterogeneity
to $\alpha\!=\!0.01$ degrades \OrbitALIF{}-SNN by only
$-0.126$\,dB ($21.886\!\to\!21.760$). \textsc{FedBN}-local
normalization absorbs the additional skew without re-tuning. The
$2.86$\,dB residual centralized-to-federated gap on PlainUNet
reflects the difficulty of the partition itself, where $30$ of $50$
satellites hold only the five-tile floor.

% Section V: Conclusion

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented \OrbitALIF{}, the first end-to-end framework that
performs both onboard training and onboard inference for optical
cloud removal on a Walker-Star LEO constellation. The architecture
is organized around three principles---multi-domain decomposition,
identity-preserving modulation, and progressive coupling---within
an SNN-sparsity foundation. The centralized $2.30$\,M-parameter
backbone reaches $25.37$/$23.20$\,dB on CUHK-CR1/CR2 (exceeding the
PlainUNet baseline by $+0.50$/$+0.16$\,dB and sitting on the
PSNR-per-parameter Pareto front of published cloud-removal models
on the CUHK-CR1 benchmark), and cuts per-image inference energy
from $20.75$\,mJ to $0.287$\,mJ on neuromorphic substrates
($72.3\!\times$ reduction, $1.21\!\times$ on general-purpose CMOS). Under FedBN-on-SNN with $\alpha\!=\!0.1$,
\OrbitALIF{}-ANN reaches $22.679$\,dB ($+0.667$\,dB over PlainUNet)
and \OrbitALIF{}-SNN reaches $21.886$\,dB (within $0.13$\,dB), with
only $0.126$\,dB further degradation when heterogeneity tightens
tenfold. Component ablation confirms the architecture is a
co-designed whole.

Future directions include closing the centralized-to-federated
gap (e.g., $2.86$\,dB on PlainUNet at $\alpha\!=\!0.1$) via partial
participation, momentum aggregation, or orbital-geometry-aware
mixing matrices, and replacing the analytic energy bracket with
hardware-measured power on neuromorphic substrates such as
Loihi-2, Speck, or Akida.
