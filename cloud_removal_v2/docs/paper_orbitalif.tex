% Abstract

\begin{abstract}
Low-Earth-orbit (LEO) optical satellites continuously acquire Earth
imagery, but cloud contamination makes a large fraction of each pass
unusable, which delays time-critical applications such as disaster
response. Downlinking raw images for ground-side cloud removal is
bottlenecked by narrow contact windows and limited inter-satellite
link bandwidth. Performing cloud removal onboard closes this gap, yet
onboard deployment must simultaneously respect four hard constraints.
The satellite power budget allows only tens of watts for compute,
inter-plane links are intermittent, the Walker-Star topology
precludes global synchronization, and data heterogeneity at the
source level differs across orbital planes.

We present \OrbitALIF{}, the first end-to-end framework that performs
both onboard training and onboard inference for optical cloud removal.
A $2.30$\,M-parameter spiking neural network backbone is coupled with
a FedBN-on-SNN aggregation protocol that runs over intra-plane ring
AllReduce and inter-plane Gossip mixing. On CUHK-CR1, \OrbitALIF{}
reaches $25.37$\,dB PSNR while reducing per-image inference energy
from $20.75$\,mJ for an ANN baseline to $0.287$\,mJ on neuromorphic
hardware, a $72.3$-fold reduction. Under Dirichlet ($\alpha\!=\!0.1$)
source heterogeneity on a $5\!\times\!10$ constellation, the
architectural advantage carries over to the federated setting and
stays within $0.13$\,dB of the ANN counterpart, degrading only
$0.126$\,dB when heterogeneity tightens tenfold.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
Spiking neural networks, optical cloud removal, decentralized
federated learning, onboard satellite computing.
\end{IEEEkeywords}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Low-Earth-orbit (LEO) optical satellites such as Sentinel-2 and
Landsat-9 continuously image the Earth surface and feed applications
ranging from rapid disaster response~\cite{ref:earthobs2} to
environmental monitoring and precision
agriculture~\cite{ref:earthobs1}. A persistent obstacle in this
pipeline is cloud contamination. Multi-year MODIS climatology reports
that about $67\%$ of the Earth surface is covered by cloud at any
given moment~\cite{ref:king2013cloud}, and a substantial fraction of
every acquired tile is routinely discarded as
unusable~\cite{ref:cuhkcr,ref:ebel2021multisensor}. Cloud removal
seeks to recover the underlying surface from a cloudy observation,
which is a dense per-pixel regression task rather than a categorical
prediction~\cite{ref:cuhkcr}. The conventional pipeline runs in two
stages. Each satellite stores raw imagery, downlinks it during one
of the few daily ground-station contact windows, and a server cluster
performs cloud removal in
batch~\cite{ref:earthobs1,ref:onboardfl-bottleneck}. Three properties
of LEO operation make this pipeline a bottleneck. Contact windows are
short, often under ten minutes per pass for a given
station~\cite{ref:onboardfl-bottleneck}. Inter-satellite link and
downlink bandwidth are limited compared with terrestrial
networks~\cite{ref:spacecpn1}. End-to-end latency from acquisition to
actionable product, typically measured in hours, is incompatible with
disaster-response decisions that must be made in
minutes~\cite{ref:earthobs2}.

Moving cloud removal onboard removes the downlink and the multi-hour
latency, but the deployment must simultaneously respect four
constraints that arise from the physics of LEO operation.

\textbf{Power.} A typical LEO Earth-observation satellite carries a
total bus power budget of $100$ to $300$\,W~\cite{ref:spacecpn1}.
After subtracting attitude control, thermal management,
communications, and the imaging payload, the budget available to
onboard computation is on the order of tens of watts, and it shrinks
further during the eclipse phase when the spacecraft runs on
battery. Ground-class ANN inference at the hundreds-of-watts scale
is not sustainable in this regime.

\textbf{Link.} Inter-satellite link (ISL) bandwidth is typically in
the $10$ to $100$\,Mbps range, one to two orders of magnitude below
terrestrial local-area
networks~\cite{ref:spacecpn1,ref:onboardfl-bottleneck}.
Ground-station downlink windows are similarly narrow, so any scheme
that depends on a central parameter server or on frequent
satellite--ground exchanges is impractical.

\textbf{Topology.} Walker-Star constellations exhibit asymmetric
connectivity~\cite{ref:walker1984}. Same-plane satellites maintain
quasi-continuous links, while inter-plane links are available only
during brief polar-region encounters and are directionally
constrained. A globally synchronous aggregation step is therefore not
realisable, and only locally mixed schemes such as Gossip remain
feasible.

\textbf{Heterogeneity.} Different orbital planes systematically cover
different latitudes, and consequently observe different
cloud-thickness climatologies, solar-zenith distributions, and sensor
radiometric drifts. Each satellite therefore holds a non-IID local
dataset whose distribution shift is at the source level (sensor and
atmospheric conditions), distinct from the class-label imbalance that
dominates the conventional federated-learning
literature~\cite{ref:hsu2019}.

These four constraints are coupled. A practical onboard
cloud-removal system must co-design the model, the aggregation
protocol, and the task formulation.

Existing approaches each address a subset of the four constraints
but fail to satisfy them jointly.

\textbf{Direct deployment of a ground-trained ANN.} Lifting a
state-of-the-art cloud-removal model~\cite{ref:cuhkcr} onto the
satellite addresses none of the four constraints. A single forward
pass on a $64\!\times\!64$ patch costs roughly $4.5$\,GMACs, or
$20.75$\,mJ at the $4.6$\,pJ/MAC of $45$\,nm
CMOS~\cite{ref:horowitz2014}, which exceeds the watt-level onboard
compute envelope at modest revisit rates and leaves data
heterogeneity untreated.

\textbf{Single-satellite local training.} Training only on the data
of one satellite respects the link and topology constraints by
definition, but it directly violates the heterogeneity constraint.
A satellite that primarily observes thin maritime cloud at low
latitudes never sees the optically thick polar cloud that dominates
another orbit, and the locally trained model fails to generalize.

\textbf{Centralized federated learning.} Standard
FedAvg~\cite{ref:fedavg} requires a central parameter server. In an
LEO setting the server must reside on the ground, which forces a
satellite--ground exchange every round and reintroduces the downlink
bottleneck that onboard processing was meant to eliminate.

\textbf{Existing decentralized SNN-FL.}
FLSNN~\cite{ref:flsnn} demonstrated decentralized federated SNN
training on a Walker-Star constellation, but only for image
classification. Cloud removal differs in three ways. It is dense
pixel regression rather than discrete label prediction. It requires
multi-scale texture recovery rather than compact feature extraction.
Its non-IID arises at the source level (cloud thickness and sensor
variation) rather than at the class-label level. None of these gaps
were addressed, and inference energy was not jointly optimized.

These observations point to a co-designed solution at three layers,
namely a model that respects the power constraint, an aggregation
protocol that respects the link and topology constraints, and a task
formulation that exposes source-level heterogeneity.

To meet these three layers jointly, we present \OrbitALIF{}, the
first end-to-end framework that performs both onboard training and
onboard inference for optical cloud removal. Our contributions are
threefold.
\textbf{C1: Energy-first SNN architecture.} \OrbitALIF{} is a
$2.30$\,M-parameter spiking neural network for dense pixel
restoration. On CUHK-CR1, the centralized model exceeds the
PlainUNet baseline by $+0.50$\,dB PSNR while reducing per-image inference
energy from $20.75$\,mJ to $0.287$\,mJ on neuromorphic substrates
such as Loihi-2~\cite{ref:loihi}, a $72.3$-fold reduction, with a
conservative bound of $1.21$-fold even on general-purpose CMOS
without neuromorphic acceleration.
\textbf{C2: Onboard federated cloud-removal pipeline.} We formulate
optical cloud removal as a decentralized onboard federated learning
problem over a $5\!\times\!10$ Walker-Star constellation, with
source-level Dirichlet heterogeneity ($\alpha\!=\!0.1$). Aggregation
couples intra-plane ring AllReduce with single-step inter-plane
Gossip, and we extend FedBN~\cite{ref:fedbn} to the SNN setting
(FedBN-on-SNN)
by keeping every BN-tagged parameter local, including spikingjelly
TDBN threshold parameters.
\textbf{C3: Protocol robustness under heterogeneity.} Under
FedBN-on-SNN, \OrbitALIF{}-SNN stays within $0.13$\,dB of its ANN
counterpart, and tightening source heterogeneity tenfold to
$\alpha\!=\!0.01$ degrades PSNR by only $0.126$\,dB, demonstrating
that local normalization absorbs source skew without protocol
re-tuning.

\section{System Model}\label{sec:sysmodel}

\subsection{Constellation and Problem Setup}\label{sec:sysmodel-setup}

We instantiate the four constraints of Section~\ref{sec:intro} on a
Walker-Star LEO constellation of $N\!=\!5$ orbital planes, each with
$K\!=\!10$ satellites and a total of $NK\!=\!50$
nodes~\cite{ref:walker1984,ref:flsnn}. Within each plane the
satellites maintain a stable along-track formation, so intra-plane
inter-satellite links (ISLs) are quasi-continuous. Adjacent planes
only meet over the polar regions, and we model the resulting
connectivity as a length-$N$ chain in which each plane has at most
two neighbours. This asymmetric topology is the physical reason
aggregation must be split into an intra-plane stage and an
inter-plane stage in Section~\ref{sec:sysmodel-procedure}.

\textbf{Local data and global objective.} Each satellite $s_{i,k}$
holds a local dataset $\mathcal{D}_{i,k}$ of paired cloudy/clear
image tiles and participates in training without uploading raw
observations. Let $g_x$ denote \OrbitALIF{} parameterized by
$x\!\in\!\mathbb{R}^d$ and $\ell$ the per-pixel restoration loss
defined in Section~\ref{sec:framework-loss}. The decentralized
objective is to minimize the uniform average of per-satellite
empirical losses,
\begin{equation}
\min_{x \in \mathbb{R}^d}\;
f(x)
\;=\;
\frac{1}{NK}\sum_{i,k}
\frac{1}{|\mathcal{D}_{i,k}|}
\!\!\sum_{(c,y)\in\mathcal{D}_{i,k}}\!\!
\ell\!\left(g_x(c),y\right).
\label{eq:globalobj}
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:globalobj} is the standard cross-silo federated
objective~\cite{ref:fedavg}, except that no central aggregator
exists and exchanges occur only along the constellation graph.

\textbf{Source-level heterogeneity model.} Different orbital planes
see different cloud climatologies. We capture this by a
source-level Dirichlet partition with $S\!=\!2$ domains, namely
CUHK-CR1 (thin cloud) and CUHK-CR2 (thick cloud).
Following~\cite{ref:hsu2019}, for each source $s$ we draw a
partition vector
$\mathbf{q}^{(s)}\!\sim\!\Dir(\alpha\,\mathbf{1}_{NK})$ over the
$NK$ satellites, and satellite $s_{i,k}$ then receives fraction
$q^{(s)}_{i,k}$ of the source-$s$ tiles, with a minimum of five
tiles per satellite enforced by post-hoc rebalancing. Smaller
$\alpha$ produces more skewed assignments. Under $\alpha\!=\!0.1$,
$30$ of $50$ satellites hold only the five-tile floor while three
satellites hold $\{117,118,118\}$ tiles, which mirrors the empirical
tail of real per-orbit cloud-type concentration. This setting is
aggressive feature shift in the FedBN sense~\cite{ref:fedbn}, and
Section~\ref{sec:exp-federated} also reports $\alpha\!=\!0.01$ as a
stress test.

\subsection{Decentralized Training Protocol}\label{sec:sysmodel-procedure}

The protocol must respect the intra-plane / inter-plane asymmetry
identified in Section~\ref{sec:sysmodel-setup} and the BN locality
required by source-level heterogeneity. Training proceeds for
$R\!=\!200$ rounds, and each round executes four steps in sequence.

\textbf{(S1) Local update.} Each satellite performs $E\!=\!2$
AdamW steps on its local dataset $\mathcal{D}_{i,k}$, producing
$x_{i,k}^{(r,E)}$. No exchange occurs in this step.

\textbf{(S2) Intra-plane ring AllReduce.} Same-plane satellites
share quasi-continuous links, so plane $i$ runs an exact
ring-AllReduce over its $K$ members and replaces each local copy
with the plane average,
\begin{equation}
x_i^{(r+\frac{1}{2})}
\;=\;
\frac{1}{K}\sum_{k \in \mathcal{K}_i} x_{i,k}^{(r,E)}.
\label{eq:intraplane}
\end{equation}
After (S2) every satellite within a plane carries identical non-BN
weights, so the inter-plane stage can treat each plane as a single
logical node.

\textbf{(S3) Inter-plane Gossip.} The chain topology only allows
one Gossip step per round between adjacent planes. We use the
standard Metropolis mixing matrix~\cite{ref:gossip-boyd},
\begin{equation}
x_i^{(r+1)}
\;=\;
\sum_{j \in \mathcal{N}} W_{ij}\, x_j^{(r+\frac{1}{2})},
\quad i \in \mathcal{N},
\label{eq:gossip}
\end{equation}
with $W_{ij}\!=\!1/(\max(d_i,d_j)\!+\!1)$ for adjacent planes
($d_i$ is the chain-degree of node $i$) and
$W_{ii}\!=\!1\!-\!\sum_{j\sim i} W_{ij}$. For the $N\!=\!5$ chain
this gives off-diagonals $\tfrac{1}{3}$ and self-weights
$\{\tfrac{2}{3},\tfrac{1}{3},\tfrac{1}{3},\tfrac{1}{3},\tfrac{2}{3}\}$.
$W$ is doubly stochastic with spectral gap
$1\!-\!\lambda_2(W)\!\approx\!0.13$, the slow mixing rate inherent
to chain topologies of length $5$.

\textbf{(S4) Intra-plane broadcast.} Plane $i$ broadcasts
$x_i^{(r+1)}$ to its $K$ members, restoring the property that each
plane carries one shared weight set at the start of round $r+1$.

\textbf{FedBN-on-SNN.} Steps (S2) and (S3) only mix \emph{shared}
parameters. Every BN-tagged tensor stays local~\cite{ref:fedbn}.
The BN tag covers the affine pair $(\gamma,\beta)$, the running
mean and variance, and---specific to our spiking
backbone---the threshold parameters of spikingjelly's
Threshold-Dependent BN (TDBN). Keeping these local lets each
satellite's normalization absorb the cloud-thickness and
radiometric drift it actually observes, which is the source-level
heterogeneity introduced in Section~\ref{sec:sysmodel-setup}. Two
implementation details matter in practice. First, the BN tag is
matched on parameter-name regex over the model state dictionary,
covering torch BN, GroupNorm, and TDBN simultaneously. Second,
spikingjelly modules expose non-tensor entries (e.g., the membrane
potential $v\!=\!0$ scalar and uninitialized spike caches) inside
their state dict, and aggregation skips these entries to avoid
silent dtype errors.

\textbf{Communication cost.} Per-round inter-plane traffic equals
$4\!\cdot\!d_{\rm shared}\!\cdot\!2\!\cdot\!(N\!-\!1)$ bytes (fp32
shared weights, both directions, $N\!-\!1\!=\!4$ chain edges). The
$R\!=\!200$-round cumulative volume is reported in
Table~\ref{tab:federated}.

\section{The \OrbitALIF{} Framework}\label{sec:framework}

\subsection{Design Principles and Architectural Overview}
\label{sec:framework-principles}

Cloud removal places two opposing pressures on the model.
High-fidelity dense pixel regression demands capacity for
multi-scale textures, which favours large ANN restoration
networks~\cite{ref:cuhkcr} but inflates inference energy. Onboard
deployment demands sparse event-driven computation to fit a
watt-level power envelope, which favours binary-spike
SNNs~\cite{ref:flsnn,ref:esdnet} but caps per-step expressivity at
one bit. We retain SNN sparsity as the energy foundation, and
within that constraint we organize the architecture around three
principles. Each principle is motivated by a concrete failure mode
of single-domain or single-branch SNN designs, and each has
appeared in isolation in prior restoration work but, to our
knowledge, has not been jointly invoked.

\textbf{P1. Multi-domain decomposition.} Cloud artefacts behave
differently in different representations of the same image. In the
raw pixel grid they appear as large, smooth regions. In the Fourier
amplitude spectrum they concentrate in a mid-frequency band. Along
the time axis of a spike sequence they change little between steps.
A network that only operates in one of these views, as a stack of
pure spatial convolutions does, captures only one slice of the
cloud signature. We therefore let each module decompose its input
in its own native domain (LIF in the value domain, PixelShuffle-LIF
in the spatial-block domain, FreqMLP in the frequency domain,
temporal-attention in the time domain), and combine the resulting
components in a downstream fusion stage.

\textbf{P2. Identity-preserving modulation.} Stacking new modules
on top of a partially-trained backbone is hazardous. A standard
residual addition $\mathbf{x}+\Phi(\mathbf{x})$ has no learnable
amplitude, so a new branch contributes from epoch one whether or
not it is helpful, and a poorly initialized branch can break a
previously-trained checkpoint. We give every modulation block the
form $\mathbf{x}\!\mapsto\!\mathbf{x}+\gamma\,(\Phi(\mathbf{x})-\mathbf{x})$,
a pass-through plus a learnable correction. The scale $\gamma$ is
initialized at zero, so at training start the module is exactly
the identity map. Uncorrupted image regions pass through
unmodified, and modules can be added without collapsing the rest
of the network.

\textbf{P3. Progressive coupling.} Two parallel branches sharing
the same input can compete unstably during early training, and
external warm-up schedules are brittle and dataset-specific. We
instead bake the curriculum into the architecture: every
cross-branch gate is initialized so the cross-talk path is
initially closed. The cross-scale gate of \DFRB{}, the residual
scale of \SHAM{}, and the FFT-magnitude scale of FreqMLP all start
from zero. Single-branch features mature first, and inter-branch
coupling opens gradually as the gates learn during normal training.

The backbone follows a three-level U-shaped encoder--decoder.
Given a cloudy RGB tile
$\mathbf{c}\!\in\!\mathbb{R}^{3\times H\times W}$, it predicts the
cloud-free image as a global residual,
\begin{equation}
\hat{\mathbf{y}}
\;=\;
\mathbf{c}\;+\;
\mathrm{Conv}\!\left(\overline{\mathrm{Net}(\mathbf{c})}^{(T)}\right),
\label{eq:globalresidual}
\end{equation}
where $T\!=\!4$ is the spike time horizon and
$\overline{(\cdot)}^{(T)}$ averages over time. The onboard variant
uses base channel width $C\!=\!24$ and has approximately
$2.30$\,M parameters. Figure~\ref{fig:overview} shows the system
overview, and Table~\ref{tab:contribution} summarizes which
principle each module instantiates. No single module satisfies all
of them, and the integration in \OrbitALIF{} is the contribution
of the remaining subsections of Section~\ref{sec:framework}.

\subsection{Neuron Scale: Quantized Spike Activation}
\label{sec:framework-neuron}

Onboard inference must be sparse to fit the satellite power
envelope, but the simplest sparse activation, the binary LIF spike,
encodes only one bit per step. That coarseness is acceptable when
the downstream task is classification, the regime in which
FLSNN~\cite{ref:flsnn} operates, but is too coarse for the dense
pixel regression of cloud removal, where each output location
needs a graded amplitude. Existing SNN restoration work such as
ESDNet~\cite{ref:esdnet} accepts the binary output and compensates
with deeper or wider networks, which erodes the energy advantage
the sparsity was meant to deliver. ANN-to-SNN
conversion~\cite{ref:rueckauer2017} preserves amplitude but loses
strict event-driven sparsity. We need a neuron whose output is
sparse enough that synaptic operations can be skipped on
zero-valued operands, yet expressive enough that one or two
non-zero outputs already carry an amplitude.

The five-level quantized spike (5QS) clamps the membrane potential
$u_t$ of a soft-reset LIF neuron to $[0,4]$, rounds, and normalizes
by four,
\begin{equation}
o_t \;=\; \frac{\mathrm{round}(\mathrm{clamp}(u_t,0,4))}{4}\;\in\;
\Big\{0,\,\tfrac{1}{4},\,\tfrac{1}{2},\,\tfrac{3}{4},\,1\Big\}.
\label{eq:5qs-forward}
\end{equation}
The leak rate is $\tau\!=\!0.25$ and the previous spike is
subtracted before the leak, giving
$u_t\!=\!(u_{t-1}\!-\!o_{t-1})\tau+i_t$. Synaptic operations fire
only when $o_t\!>\!0$, which preserves event-driven sparsity, while
the four non-zero levels carry two bits per step instead of one.
Training uses the standard straight-through surrogate $\partial
o_t/\partial u_t \approx \tfrac{1}{4}\cdot\mathbb{1}\{0\!\le\!u_t\!\le\!4\}$.
This neuron is the SNN-sparsity foundation that all three
architectural principles operate within, and is the activation-level
expression of the energy claim made in Section~\ref{sec:intro}.

\subsection{Block Scale: Multi-Domain Decomposition}
\label{sec:framework-block}

Cloud artefacts are not a single-frequency phenomenon. Their
boundaries carry high spatial frequency, the cloudy interior is a
low-spatial-frequency colour cast, and the amplitude spectrum of
the cloudy patch occupies a band that overlaps with the surface
texture below. A single $3\!\times\!3$ convolution path cannot
disentangle these components, since it weights every frequency
band uniformly. We instantiate principle P1 at the block scale by
stacking two complementary decompositions, one in the
pixel-value/spatial domain (\DFRB{}) and one in the time/frequency
domain (\SHAM{}).

\textbf{Dual-Frequency Residual Block (\DFRB{}).} \DFRB{} routes
its input through two cascaded groups. Group~1 thresholds the
input $\mathbf{X}$ in the value domain via a 5QS-LIF neuron,
producing a high-component $\mathbf{X}_h$ above threshold and a
low-component $\mathbf{X}_l\!=\!\mathbf{X}\!-\!\mathbf{X}_h$. The
two are recombined with learnable scales $\alpha_h,\alpha_l$ and a
multiplicative high-region gate that lets the original signal
re-enter where the high-component fires,
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{Z}_1
\;=\;
\mathrm{TDBN}\bigl(\mathrm{Conv}(
\alpha_h\mathbf{X}_h+\alpha_l\mathbf{X}_l+\mathbf{X}\odot\mathbf{X}_h)\bigr).
\label{eq:dfrb-g1}
\end{equation}
Group~2 applies the same template in the spatial-block domain via
a $2\!\times$ PixelShuffle-LIF, yielding a between-block
high-component $\mathbf{Z}_{2h}$. A cross-scale gate $\sigma(g)$
with $g$ initialized at zero couples the two groups through
$\sigma(g)\,\mathbf{X}_h\!\odot\!\mathbf{Z}_{2h}$, instantiating P3
so that each group first matures in isolation. The block closes
with a shortcut $\mathrm{Conv}(\mathbf{X})$, an additive global
residual, and a \SHAM{} tail.

\textbf{Spectral-Spatial Hybrid Attention (\SHAM{}).} Following the
FSTA-SNN attention design~\cite{ref:fstasnn2025}, \SHAM{} re-weights
spike features along the two axes that \DFRB{} does not touch: the
time axis and the Fourier amplitude spectrum. A temporal
amplitude attention (TAA) reweights the $T$ spike steps with a
sigmoid gate. A spatial frequency attention (SFA) takes the 2D real
FFT of the temporal mean, modulates the magnitude with a
\texttt{FreqMLP}, reconstructs via inverse FFT (phase preserved so
that spatial structure is not destroyed), and projects through a
$7\!\times\!7$ convolution with a sigmoid head producing a spatial
mask. The two branches are composed through an outer P2-style gate,
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{SHAM}(\mathbf{X})
\;=\;
\mathbf{X}+\sigma(\eta)\bigl(\mathrm{SFA}(\mathrm{TAA}(\mathbf{X}))-\mathbf{X}\bigr),
\label{eq:sham}
\end{equation}
with $\eta$ initialized at zero so the module is identity at
training start. \SHAM{} embeds at the tail of every \DFRB{}.

\subsection{Network Scale: Gated Fusion and Hyper-Block}
\label{sec:framework-network}

The network-scale operators address two failure modes that
block-scale operators cannot fix. First, encoder skip connections
in a U-Net carry locally accurate textures but also locally
corrupted features in thick-cloud regions, and a hard skip addition
mixes both indiscriminately. Second, the highest-resolution
encoder--decoder level needs a global receptive field to
hallucinate cloud-occluded surface from distant unoccluded context,
which a stack of $3\!\times\!3$ convolutions cannot deliver at
reasonable cost.

\textbf{Adaptive Gated Fusion (\AGFM{}).} \AGFM{} produces a
spatially varying gate from the concatenated decoder feature
$\mathbf{d}$ and the encoder skip $\mathbf{e}$, fuses them, and
routes the result through a 5QS-LIF activation and TDBN,
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{f}
\;=\;
\mathrm{TDBN}\bigl(\mathrm{LIF}(\boldsymbol{\gamma}\odot\mathbf{d}+(1-\boldsymbol{\gamma})\odot\mathbf{e})\bigr),
\label{eq:agfm}
\end{equation}
with $\boldsymbol{\gamma}\!=\!\sigma\bigl(\mathrm{Conv}_{1\times 1}([\mathbf{d};\mathbf{e}]_C)\bigr)$.
The gate is per-pixel and per-channel, so the network can trust the
decoder feature in heavily corrupted regions and the encoder skip
elsewhere. The pre-sigmoid logits of $\boldsymbol{\gamma}$ are
initialized at zero, so $\boldsymbol{\gamma}$ starts at $0.5$ (a
balanced fusion of the two paths) and progressively differentiates
as training proceeds, which instantiates P3. \AGFM{} is inserted at
the first and second multi-scale levels, while the deepest level
uses a direct encoder--decoder connection.

\textbf{Spectro-Temporal Spike Hyper-Block (\SSHB{}).} \SSHB{} is
the top-level block. It first applies \DFRB{} for local
multi-domain decomposition, then expands the time axis from $T$ to
$Tr^2\!=\!16$ via a $2\!\times$ PixelUnshuffle so that the LIF
neuron's temporal memory bridges spatial neighbours, applies a
temporal-channel attention (TCAM) over the expanded steps,
compresses back to $T$ steps with a Conv3D, refines with two
stacked $[\mathrm{LIF}\!\to\!\mathrm{Conv}\!\to\!\mathrm{TDBN}\!\to\!\mathrm{TCS\text{-}Att}]$
units, upsamples by $2\!\times$, and skip-adds the initial \DFRB{}
output. A FreqMLP (FSE), adapted from the frequency-domain MLP of
DarkIR~\cite{ref:darkir2024}, closes the block. Its magnitude
scale $\gamma_F$ is initialized at zero so that the FFT branch
satisfies P2 at training start, and it provides a global receptive
field at $O(N\log N)$ rather than the $O(N^2)$ cost of a direct
non-local block,
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{SSHB}(\mathbf{X})
=\;&\mathrm{FSE}\circ\mathrm{TCS\text{-}Att}\circ
(\cdot{+}\mathrm{DFRB}(\mathbf{X}))\\
&\circ\mathrm{Up}_{2}\circ\mathrm{Refine}\circ\mathrm{Cmp}\\
&\circ\mathrm{TCAM}\circ\mathrm{LIF}\circ\mathrm{PU}_{2}\circ\mathrm{DFRB}(\mathbf{X}).
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:sshb}
\end{equation}
The PixelUnshuffle / Compress pair is the network-scale instance
of P1: the same signal is decomposed into a longer
spike-temporal sequence, processed there, and recombined back to
the spatial domain.

\subsection{Training Objective and Protocol Interface}
\label{sec:framework-loss}

\OrbitALIF{} is trained with a composite Charbonnier and SSIM loss,
\begin{equation}
\ell(\hat{\mathbf{y}},\mathbf{y})
=
\frac{1}{|\Omega|}\!\sum_{p\in\Omega}
\sqrt{(\hat{\mathbf{y}}_p-\mathbf{y}_p)^2+\varepsilon^2}
+\lambda\bigl(1-\mathrm{SSIM}(\hat{\mathbf{y}},\mathbf{y})\bigr),
\label{eq:loss}
\end{equation}
with $\varepsilon\!=\!10^{-3}$ and $\lambda\!=\!0.1$. The
Charbonnier term handles cloud-occlusion outliers more robustly
than $L^2$, and the SSIM term preserves cloud-boundary sharpness
and structural consistency that pixelwise losses tend to
oversmooth.

\textbf{Protocol interface.} The same backbone is trained both
centrally (Section~\ref{sec:exp-centralized}) and federatedly
(Section~\ref{sec:exp-federated}). In the federated regime, every
local copy minimizes Eq.~\eqref{eq:loss} on its own data, and the
constellation pipeline of Section~\ref{sec:sysmodel} mediates
parameter exchange. \textsc{FedBN}~\cite{ref:fedbn} keeps every
BN-tagged tensor local, including TDBN threshold parameters, which
absorbs the source-level cloud-thickness and radiometric drift
introduced in Section~\ref{sec:sysmodel-setup}.
Gossip~\cite{ref:gossip-boyd} on the chain topology replaces the
central parameter server. Both choices are taken from prior work
and are not claimed as new FL algorithms. The novelty at this
interface is the joint application to a spiking restoration
backbone, in particular the explicit handling of TDBN thresholds
and of spikingjelly's non-tensor state-dict entries during
aggregation.

\section{Experiments}\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}\label{sec:exp-setup}

\textbf{Datasets.} CUHK-CR~\cite{ref:cuhkcr} provides paired
cloudy/clear Sentinel-2 RGB tiles. CR1 (thin cloud) and CR2 (thick
cloud) define the two Dirichlet source domains. Training and
evaluation use $64\!\times\!64$ random and centre crops
respectively.

\textbf{Models.} Three matched-protocol backbones share the same
aggregation pipeline. \OrbitALIF{}-SNN uses \FQS{}-LIF, TDBN,
$T\!=\!4$, and base width $C\!=\!24$ for $2.30$\,M parameters.
\OrbitALIF{}-ANN replaces every \FQS{}-LIF with ReLU and every TDBN
with BN, keeping the same dataflow. PlainUNet is a vanilla U-Net
baseline used for ablation reference.

\textbf{Training.} Centralized runs use $400$ epochs of AdamW with
$10^{-3}\!\to\!10^{-7}$ cosine learning rate, three warmup epochs,
batch size four, and gradient clipping at $\|g\|_2\!\le\!1$.
Ablation runs use $300$ epochs. Federated runs use $R\!=\!200$
rounds of $E\!=\!2$ local steps under Dirichlet
$\alpha\!=\!0.1$. We report PSNR, SSIM, cumulative inter-plane
communication in MB, and wall time.

\textbf{Energy measurement.} A forward hook on every
\texttt{Conv2d} and \texttt{Linear} records the per-call MAC count
and input non-zero rate $r_\ell$, accumulated over $32$ CUHK-CR1
test patches at $64\!\times\!64$. Two per-operation constants are
taken from the literature: $4.6$\,pJ/MAC for the ANN baseline
(Horowitz, $45$\,nm CMOS~\cite{ref:horowitz2014}) and $77$\,fJ/SOP
for SNN deployment on neuromorphic substrates such as
Loihi-2~\cite{ref:loihi}, the value adopted by
FLSNN~\cite{ref:flsnn} and ESDNet~\cite{ref:esdnet}. Per-image SNN
energy is $\sum_\ell \mathrm{MAC}_\ell\,r_\ell\,e_{\rm op}$.

\subsection{Per-Image Inference Energy}\label{sec:exp-energy}

The energy claim of Section~\ref{sec:intro} can be misreported in
two ways. The number of synaptic operations a trained SNN actually
executes is much smaller than the worst-case sparse arithmetic
that pure FLOPs counting suggests. The per-operation energy
constant also differs by roughly $60\!\times$ between standard
CMOS and neuromorphic substrates, so a single multiplier cannot
cover both deployment scenarios. We therefore measure the real
per-layer non-zero rates on CR1 (Section~\ref{sec:exp-setup}) and
report a dual bound, with the lower bound matching neuromorphic
deployment and the upper bound assuming no neuromorphic
acceleration is available.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{fig6_energy_bars.pdf}
  \caption{Per-image estimated inference energy on a
  $64\!\times\!64$ CUHK-CR1 centre patch. ANN baseline at
  $4.6$\,pJ/MAC. \OrbitALIF{} is reported as a dual bound, with the
  lower bound at $77$\,fJ/SOP for neuromorphic substrates and the
  upper bound charging every non-zero MAC at the full ANN rate.
  Multipliers vs.\ ANN are annotated above each bar.}
  \label{fig:energy}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Total energy.} Figure~\ref{fig:energy} reports per-image
bounds. The ANN baseline consumes $20.75$\,mJ over the
$4.51$\,GMAC inference. \OrbitALIF{} consumes $0.287$\,mJ in
the lower bound (Loihi-2, Speck, Akida class substrates), a
$72.3\!\times\!$ reduction, and $17.14$\,mJ in the upper bound
(general-purpose CMOS without neuromorphic acceleration), a
$1.21\!\times\!$ reduction. Across CR1 and CR2 inferences the
measured non-zero MAC ratios are $\bar{r}_{\rm CR1}\!=\!0.826$
and $\bar{r}_{\rm CR2}\!=\!0.823$, indistinguishable at two decimal
places.

\textbf{Per-layer firing rates.} Forward hooks on the LIF and
\FQS{} modules show $20$\,--\,$70\,\%$ non-zero activation along
the analysis path. \FQS{} layers run higher because four of five
quantization levels are non-zero by construction.

\textbf{Disclosure.} The MAC counts and per-layer rates are real
measurements. The per-operation energy constants are taken from
the literature listed in Section~\ref{sec:exp-setup}.
Hardware-measured power on neuromorphic platforms is deferred to
deployment validation.

\subsection{Centralized Cloud-Removal Performance}\label{sec:exp-centralized}

The energy savings of Section~\ref{sec:exp-energy} are only useful
if restoration quality holds up. Spiking restoration networks
frequently underperform their ANN counterparts at the same
parameter count~\cite{ref:esdnet}, which raises the question of
whether a similar gap appears here. We train \OrbitALIF{} centrally
under the same optimization protocol as the PlainUNet baseline and
read off CR1 and CR2 numbers.

Under AdamW with $10^{-3}\!\to\!10^{-7}$ cosine over $400$ epochs
and batch size four, \OrbitALIF{} reaches $25.374$\,dB on CUHK-CR1
and $23.197$\,dB on CUHK-CR2, exceeding the PlainUNet baseline by
$+0.502$\,dB on CR1 and $+0.157$\,dB on CR2 despite sparse spike
activations and TDBN-quantized intermediate features. The narrower
CR2 margin reflects the harder restoration ceiling under fully
opaque cloud cover, an effect we revisit in the qualitative results
(Section~\ref{sec:exp-qualitative}). The CR1 gain is architectural,
not a side effect of any one component: the ablations of
Section~\ref{sec:exp-ablation} show that disabling either \SHAM{}
or the dual-group \DFRB{} removes the gain entirely
(Tab.~\ref{tab:ablation}, rows B1--B2).

\subsection{Component Ablation}\label{sec:exp-ablation}

The previous subsection claimed that the CR1 gain is architectural
rather than driven by a single dominant module. We test this by
disabling one \OrbitALIF{} component at a time and retraining on
CR1 for $300$ epochs. B1 replaces every \SHAM{} block with the
identity. B2 replaces every \DFRB{} with a single-group residual
block that keeps only the temporal LIF group, removing the
PixelShuffle-LIF spatial path and the cross-scale gate $\sigma(g)$.
B3 replaces \FQS{} with a binary spike, keeping the surrogate
gradient window $[0,4]$ and $1/4$ scale unchanged so that only
the forward quantization granularity changes. \AGFM{} is excluded
as its $1\!\times\!1$ gate at L1/L2 contributes under one percent
of parameters.

\textbf{Component ranking.} Figure~\ref{fig:ablation} visualizes
the per-component drops, which in Tab.~\ref{tab:ablation} order as
\SHAM{} ($-0.597$\,dB), then the
dual-group \DFRB{} ($-0.516$\,dB), then \FQS{} ($-0.302$\,dB),
which matches the design rationale of
Section~\ref{sec:framework}. \SHAM{} contributes orthogonal
recalibration along the temporal-amplitude and frequency-spatial
axes that no other module recovers. The dual-group \DFRB{}
contributes the spatial high-frequency path that complements the
temporal LIF group. \FQS{} contributes the smallest drop,
consistent with its role as the activation-level expression of SNN
sparsity rather than a feature-extraction component.

\textbf{Co-design evidence.} Both B1 ($24.777$\,dB) and B2
($24.858$\,dB) fall below PlainUNet at $24.872$\,dB. Removing one
component therefore breaks \OrbitALIF{} more than reverting to a
plain ANN. The $+0.502$\,dB CR1 margin is not a sum of independent
component gains but a synergy that requires all components to
operate together. Only B3 stays above PlainUNet, indicating that
\FQS{} is a refinement rather than a load-bearing module.

\subsection{Decentralized Federated Cloud-Removal}\label{sec:exp-federated}

The architectural advantage measured in
Section~\ref{sec:exp-centralized} was obtained under centralized
training with full data access. The harder question is whether it
survives the decentralized setting introduced in
Section~\ref{sec:sysmodel-procedure}, where each satellite sees
only its source-Dirichlet share of the data and exchanges
parameters with a chain-Gossip topology. We deploy three
matched-protocol backbones on the $5\!\times\!10\!=\!50$-satellite
Walker-Star constellation under $\alpha\!=\!0.1$ for $R\!=\!200$
rounds. \OrbitALIF{}-SNN is the full spiking variant.
\OrbitALIF{}-ANN replaces every \FQS{}-LIF with ReLU. PlainUNet is
the ANN U-Net baseline. All three share the identical aggregation
pipeline and differ only in the per-satellite network, so any
performance gap is attributable to the backbone alone.

\textbf{Architecture survives the federated setting.}
Figure~\ref{fig:federated} plots test PSNR against communication
rounds for the three backbones. Under identical
\textsc{FedBN}\,+\,Gossip, $R\!=\!200$, $\alpha\!=\!0.1$,
\OrbitALIF{}-ANN reaches $22.679$\,dB
($0.6966$ SSIM), exceeding the PlainUNet floor of $22.012$\,dB by
$+0.667$\,dB ($+0.0147$ SSIM). This is in fact larger than the
$+0.502$\,dB centralized CR1 margin, indicating that the
architectural advantage scales with rather than diminishes under
decentralized training.

\textbf{Spiking cost is recovered in energy.} Replacing
\FQS{}-LIF with ReLU\,+\,BN costs the SNN variant $0.793$\,dB
versus the ANN variant ($21.886$ vs $22.679$, SSIM $0.6725$ vs
$0.6966$). \OrbitALIF{}-SNN still lands within $0.13$\,dB of the
ANN PlainUNet baseline despite sparse spike activations and
TDBN-quantized intermediates, and the loss is recouped in the
$1.21\!\times$ to $72.3\!\times$ inference-energy reduction
reported in Section~\ref{sec:exp-energy}.

\textbf{Heterogeneity sensitivity.} Tightening source heterogeneity
from $\alpha\!=\!0.1$ to $\alpha\!=\!0.01$ degrades
\OrbitALIF{}-SNN by only $-0.126$\,dB
($21.886\!\to\!21.760$, SSIM $0.6725\!\to\!0.6691$).
\textsc{FedBN}-local normalization therefore absorbs most of the
additional skew without re-tuning the protocol. The $2.86$\,dB gap
that remains between centralized and federated PlainUNet on CR1
($24.872\!\to\!22.012$) reflects the difficulty of the
$\alpha\!=\!0.1$ partition itself: $30$ of $50$ satellites hold
only the five-tile floor, so a single satellite cannot reconstruct
the full encoder representation in $R\!=\!200$ rounds.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented \OrbitALIF{}, the first end-to-end framework that
performs both onboard training and onboard inference for optical
cloud removal on a Walker-Star LEO constellation. The architecture
is organized around three principles---multi-domain decomposition,
identity-preserving modulation, and progressive coupling---within
an SNN-sparsity foundation that supplies the energy advantage. On
CUHK-CR1 and CUHK-CR2 the centralized $2.30$\,M-parameter backbone
reaches $25.37$/$23.20$\,dB ($0.7728$/$0.7449$ SSIM), exceeding the
PlainUNet baseline by $+0.50$\,dB on CR1 and $+0.16$\,dB on CR2,
and reduces per-image inference energy from $20.75$\,mJ for the
ANN baseline to $0.287$\,mJ on neuromorphic substrates, a
$72.3$-fold reduction with a $1.21$-fold conservative bound on
general-purpose CMOS. Under FedBN-on-SNN with Dirichlet
$\alpha\!=\!0.1$ source heterogeneity, \OrbitALIF{}-ANN reaches
$22.679$\,dB ($+0.667$\,dB over PlainUNet), \OrbitALIF{}-SNN
reaches $21.886$\,dB (within $0.13$\,dB of the ANN baseline), and
the SNN variant degrades by only $0.126$\,dB when heterogeneity
tightens tenfold to $\alpha\!=\!0.01$. A component ablation
confirms that the architecture is a co-designed whole rather than
the sum of independent module gains.

Future directions include (i)~moving from the paired-synthetic
CUHK-CR benchmark to a real on-orbit corpus that captures
instrument-specific radiometric drift, (ii)~closing the
centralized-to-federated gap (e.g., $2.86$\,dB on PlainUNet at
$\alpha\!=\!0.1$) via partial participation, momentum aggregation,
or orbital-geometry-aware mixing matrices, and (iii)~replacing the
analytic energy bracket with hardware-measured power on
neuromorphic substrates such as Loihi-2, Speck, or Akida.
